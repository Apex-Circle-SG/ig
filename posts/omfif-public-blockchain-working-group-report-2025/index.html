OMFIF Public Blockchain Working Group Report (2025)

2025-11-15

The report examines how public blockchains are evolving from experimental tools into viable components of the global financial system, while highlighting the regulatory, technological and governance challenges that must be addressed for mainstream adoption—especially by banks.

1. Need for Common Understanding & Regulatory Alignment

Regulators, technologists and financial institutions still lack a shared understanding of how public blockchains function. This creates friction in developing policy frameworks that protect market integrity while still enabling innovation. Because capital markets are globally interconnected, inconsistent regulations across jurisdictions could fragment liquidity and hinder adoption.

2. Current Regulatory Issues

Many existing regulations treat all public blockchains as inherently risky. The Basel Committee’s prudential treatment of crypto-asset exposures is highlighted as particularly problematic:

  • It categorizes most assets issued on public blockchains (even tokenized securities or stablecoins) as high-risk (risk weight 1250%).
  • This makes it cost-prohibitive for banks to engage with public blockchain-based assets.
  • Several jurisdictions—including the US—are unlikely to implement the Basel rules as written, leading to regulatory divergence.

The report argues that regulation should focus on the characteristics of the asset and the functions required for financial stability, not on the architecture of the blockchain itself.

3. Public vs Private vs Permissioned Blockchains

Traditional labels such as “public/permissionless” or “private/permissioned” no longer accurately reflect blockchain capabilities.

Technological advancements (Layer-2s, improved consensus mechanisms, token standards, validator screening tools) blur the lines between categories.
Some public blockchains now offer:

  • deterministic (instant) settlement
  • strong governance models
  • validator permissioning
  • anti-MEV (no front-running)
  • confidentiality options

The report argues that regulators should assess blockchains based on functionality, not simplistic architecture labels.

4. Functional Requirements for Regulated Financial Activity

The core of the report outlines the eight functions regulators should require for blockchain-based financial infrastructure:

  1. Accountability & Governance
    – Controls must exist at the issuer/token level; blockchain protocols themselves should not be regulated as financial infrastructure.
  2. Operational Resilience
    – Public blockchains are highly resilient, but institutions must maintain fallback plans, backup chains and legal continuity methods.
  3. Settlement Finality
    – Deterministic finality is required. Legal finality must be explicitly recognized by regulators.
  4. Throughput & Fee Stability
    – Modern blockchains can support thousands of TPS with predictable low fees.
  5. Asset Control
    – Permissioned token standards allow KYC-gated access, freezes, clawbacks, escrow, circuit-breakers and compliance logic.
  6. Confidentiality
    – Fully transparent ledgers are incompatible with institutional trading. Emerging privacy tech (e.g., zero-knowledge proofs) can solve this.
  7. Validator Screening
    – Validators are not counterparties, but regulators may require that networks demonstrate validators are known, reputable or screened.
  8. Interoperability
    – Regulators should promote common standards to avoid fragmentation and vendor lock-in. Cross-chain transfers and universal schemas are essential.

5. Banks’ Role and Barriers

Banks face more stringent regulatory requirements than fintech or crypto-native firms. Until banks can comfortably adopt blockchain infrastructure, institutional capital markets on-chain will remain limited.

6. The Role of Regulators Going Forward

Regulators should:

  • Avoid classifying blockchains themselves as financial market infrastructure.
  • Provide clear frameworks and risk-management guidelines to banks.
  • Establish sandboxes, pilot regimes and labs to test blockchain-based settlement environments.
  • Encourage shared standards (interoperability, asset schemas, legal definitions).

The report stresses that public blockchains are technologically ready for regulated financial activity. What remains is regulatory clarity and integration work.

7. Industry Contributions

Working group members (Aptos, Hedera, Ripple, Stellar, Minsait) provide insights on:

  • High-performance public chains
  • Deterministic consensus (Hashgraph)
  • Permissioned validator models
  • Privacy layers
  • Machine-readable legal standards
  • Cross-chain protocols
  • Asset schema standardization

These demonstrate the rapid evolution and maturity of public blockchain infrastructure.
