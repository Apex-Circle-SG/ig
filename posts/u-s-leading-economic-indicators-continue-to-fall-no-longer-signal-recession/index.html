U.S. Leading Economic Indicators Continue to Fall, No Longer Signal Recession

2026-05-14

The Shifting Narrative of U.S. Economic Data

For months, financial markets and economic analysts have been fixated on a single, troubling statistic: the continuous decline of the Conference Board Leading Economic Index (LEI). Historically, a sustained drop in this index has been a reliable harbinger of an impending recession. However, the current economic landscape presents a paradox that challenges traditional forecasting models. Despite the LEI registering declines for over two years, the feared contraction in the U.S. economy has failed to materialize. Instead, the nation has seen resilient job growth, steady consumer spending, and robust GDP expansion. This divergence suggests that while leading indicators are indeed falling, they may no longer signal a recession in the way they once did.

Understanding this shift is critical for investors, business leaders, and policymakers. The disconnect between the leading indicators and actual economic performance points to structural changes in the post-pandemic economy, unique supply-side dynamics, and the lagging effects of monetary policy that differ from previous cycles. As we delve deeper into the data, it becomes clear that the warning lights on the dashboard might be flashing due to a sensor glitch rather than an engine failure.

Decoding the Leading Economic Index

To understand why the signal might be misfiring, one must first understand the composition of the Leading Economic Index. Compiled by The Conference Board, the LEI is a composite of ten sub-indexes designed to predict economic turning points. These components include average weekly hours worked in manufacturing, initial claims for unemployment insurance, manufacturers’ new orders for consumer goods, and stock prices, among others. Historically, when the LEI falls for several consecutive months, a recession follows within six to eighteen months.

In the current cycle, the LEI has been in negative territory for a record-breaking duration. The primary drivers of this decline have been specific sectors that are highly sensitive to interest rates and global trade tensions. For instance, the housing sector, heavily influenced by the Federal Reserve’s aggressive rate hikes to combat inflation, has seen a significant slowdown. Similarly, manufacturing new orders have contracted amid global uncertainty. However, these sectors represent a shrinking portion of the overall U.S. economy, which is increasingly dominated by services and digital consumption.

Why the Traditional Model Is Struggling

The traditional reliance on the LEI as a crystal ball assumes that the economy operates under the same structural conditions as it did in previous decades. This assumption is currently being tested. The post-2020 economy is characterized by unique factors such as massive fiscal stimulus, pent-up consumer savings, and a labor market that remains surprisingly tight despite higher borrowing costs. Consequently, the weight of declining manufacturing orders or building permits is being offset by strength in other areas not fully captured or weighted heavily enough in the index to reflect the broader resilience.

Evidence of Economic Resilience

While the leading indicators point downward, the concurrent economic data paints a picture of durability. The U.S. labor market has remained a cornerstone of this strength. Unemployment rates have hovered near historic lows, and job creation has consistently outpaced expectations. When consumers have jobs and wages are rising, they continue to spend, driving roughly two-thirds of the U.S. economy. This consumer resilience has acted as a buffer against the headwinds predicted by the LEI.

Furthermore, Gross Domestic Product (GDP) growth has remained positive and, in several quarters, has exceeded forecasts. Businesses, particularly in the service sector, continue to invest and expand.
