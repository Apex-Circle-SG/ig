GovCon Expert Ron Lear: How to Strengthen Agile Development With CMMI

2026-05-19

The Intersection of Speed and Compliance

In the high-stakes world of government contracting (GovCon), federal agencies are often caught between two competing pressures. On one hand, there is the demand for rapid, iterative delivery of software solutions, typically satisfied through Agile development methodologies. On the other hand, there is a strict mandate for governance, risk management, and quality assurance, often associated with the Capability Maturity Model Integration (CMMI). As GovCon expert Ron Lear consistently argues, viewing these frameworks as contradictory is a fundamental mistake. When integrated correctly, CMMI provides the necessary scaffolding to make Agile projects more predictable, repeatable, and scalable in a federal environment.

The Misconception of Agile vs. CMMI

A common misconception in the engineering community is that Agile is about ‘no documentation’ and ‘fast hacking,’ while CMMI is about ‘excessive bureaucracy.’ Ron Lear clarifies that this binary thinking fails to produce desired outcomes. Agile is indeed about delivering value quickly, but it does not inherently offer guidance on how to manage enterprise-level risk or ensure that multiple teams are synchronized toward a common mission-critical goal.

CMMI, at its core, is a performance improvement framework. It is not a process in itself but a set of best practices that describe what should be done to achieve high-performance results. When organizations adopt CMMI, they are seeking to institutionalize excellence rather than relying on individual heroism, which is particularly relevant in the high-turnover environment of government contracting.

How Ron Lear Bridges the Gap

Ron Lear provides a blueprint for government contractors looking to integrate these approaches. The philosophy hinges on focusing on the intent of the CMMI practices rather than the prescriptive implementation of them. By focusing on outcome-based maturity, teams can fulfill their compliance requirements while remaining true to Agile principles.

1. Mapping Agile Ceremonies to CMMI Practices

Lear suggests that many Agile ceremonies naturally satisfy CMMI requirements if mapped correctly. For example, Sprint Retrospectives are effectively Process and Product Quality Assurance (PPQA) and Measurement and Analysis mechanisms. By intentionally documenting the outcomes of these retrospectives, organizations satisfy audit requirements without creating additional ‘process debt’ for the development team.

2. Embracing Risk Management within Epics

In CMMI, Risk Management is a formal, continuous process. In Agile, it often happens during daily stand-ups or sprint planning. Lear advises adding explicit ‘risk identification’ tasks to epic breakdowns. By institutionalizing this within the Agile backlog, the project adheres to the CMMI Maturity Level 3 requirement of proactive risk management without slowing down the development velocity.

3. Standardizing Agile Frameworks

A frequent finding in CMMI appraisals is the lack of a standardized process. If every team interprets Scrum differently, the organization cannot measure its performance effectively. Ron Lear advocates for an ‘Agile Tailoring Guide’ that defines the organization’s standard Agile approach while providing teams the flexibility to adjust specific activities based on project needs. This achieves CMMI compliance regarding Technical Solution and Product Integration while maintaining the adaptability required by Agile.

The Value Proposition for Government Agencies

For government agencies, this integrated approach provides several tangible benefits:

  • Predictability and Reliability: Federal mission success relies on predictable delivery, not just fast development.
  • Scalability: As projects grow from one team to ten, institutionalized CMMI processes provide the necessary structure to manage dependencies.
  • Auditability: Because CMMI focuses on evidence-based processes, the transition from development to production—or from development to an audit—becomes seamless.
  • Consistent Quality: By applying a maturity framework, the organization reduces variability in output, minimizing defects and technical debt.

Overcoming Cultural Resistance

Ron Lear highlights that the biggest barrier to this integration is not technical, but cultural. Software engineers often resist CMMI due to perceived overhead, while quality assurance professionals may resist Agile due to perceived lack of control. To overcome this, Lear recommends a strategy of ‘Agile-first, CMMI-aligned’ communication. Leadership must articulate that the goal is improved mission delivery, and that CMMI is simply the tool that ensures that the agility is safe, repeatable, and compliant.

Structuring Evidence for Appraisals

When preparing for a CMMI appraisal, Agile teams often panic, thinking they need to create new documents. Lear’s expert advice is to use the ‘Artifacts of Agility’ as evidence.

  • Use Jira boards and burn-down charts as evidence of Measurement and Analysis.
  • Use Definition of Done (DoD) as evidence of Technical Solution and Product Verification.
  • Use Sprint Review notes as evidence of Stakeholder Management.

By mapping existing Agile workflow artifacts directly to practice areas, teams eliminate redundant effort.

Conclusion

Strengthening Agile development with CMMI is not about imposing heavy, waterfall-like controls on high-velocity teams. As demonstrated by GovCon expert Ron Lear, it is about creating a mature infrastructure that supports Agile practices. By aligning the intent of CMMI with the methodology of Agile, federal contractors can ensure that they are not just delivering code quickly, but delivering the right solutions, predictably and within the stringent compliance standards demanded by the federal government.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does CMMI fit into an Agile Scrum environment?

CMMI fits by treating Agile ceremonies as the evidence and vehicles for CMMI practice areas. For example, sprint reviews and retrospectives satisfy requirements for measurement, quality assurance, and stakeholder communication without needing additional, separate documentation.

Does adding CMMI slow down development teams?

When implemented correctly, adhering to CMMI can actually accelerate development by reducing rework due to defects and improving the predictability of delivery. The goal is to integrate compliance into the existing workflow, not to add it on as a separate layer of bureaucracy.

What is the biggest mistake contractors make with CMMI and Agile?

The biggest mistake is attempting to maintain two separate systems: one for engineering (Agile) and one for process compliance (CMMI). This creates a ‘shadow process’ that confuses teams and leads to inefficient documentation efforts.

Do I need full CMMI certification to be effective?

While formal appraisal/certification is often a contract requirement in GovCon, the real value lies in the implementation of the CMMI best practices to improve project maturity, regardless of whether a formal appraisal is imminent.

How can I get developers to buy into CMMI?

Frame CMMI not as a compliance burden, but as a set of tools to remove blockers, clarify requirements, and ensure that their development work is valued and recognized by stakeholders through documented, evidence-based performance improvements.
