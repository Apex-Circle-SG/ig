Avalere Analysis: Why CMMI Savings May Fall Short of CBO Projections

2026-05-06

The Evolving Landscape of Medicare Innovation

For years, the Center for Medicare and Medicaid Innovation (CMMI) has been positioned as the primary engine for transforming the American healthcare delivery system. Tasked with testing innovative payment and service delivery models, CMMI aims to reduce expenditures for Medicare, Medicaid, and the Children’s Health Insurance Program (CHIP) while preserving or enhancing the quality of care. However, recent analysis from Avalere Health indicates that these ambitious goals may face significant headwinds, casting doubt on whether CMMI will generate the level of savings previously projected by the Congressional Budget Office (CBO).

Understanding the Gap Between CBO Projections and Real-World Outcomes

The Congressional Budget Office (CBO) has historically relied on optimistic modeling when forecasting the fiscal impact of CMMI initiatives. These projections often assume that payment reforms and value-based care models will systematically lower costs across the board. Avalere Health, a leading healthcare consulting firm, argues that this perspective fails to account for the complexities of provider adoption, administrative burden, and the inherent variability in clinical settings.

The Complexity of Value-Based Care Adoption

One of the primary drivers of potential savings is the shift toward value-based care. While the theoretical framework is sound—paying for outcomes rather than volume—the execution is fraught with difficulty. Providers often struggle with the transition, requiring significant capital investment in data analytics, care coordination staff, and infrastructure. These upfront costs often offset the savings generated by reduced hospitalizations or improved chronic disease management, at least in the short term.

Administrative Hurdles and Provider Burnout

Avalere suggests that the complexity of participating in CMMI models acts as a deterrent for many healthcare organizations. When administrative requirements become too onerous, providers may choose to remain in traditional fee-for-service arrangements. This creates a selection bias where only the most well-resourced organizations participate, potentially limiting the scalability of these models and preventing the widespread cost reductions the CBO originally envisioned.

Key Challenges Facing CMMI Models

Several structural and operational factors contribute to the discrepancy between projected and realized savings. Understanding these factors is essential for policymakers and stakeholders who rely on CBO data for budgetary planning.

  • Model Attrition: Many participants drop out of CMMI models when they realize the financial risk is too high or the administrative burden is unsustainable.
  • Benchmark Setting: Getting the financial benchmarks right is notoriously difficult. If benchmarks are too high, the government overpays; if they are too low, providers leave the program.
  • Lack of Infrastructure in Rural Areas: Smaller, rural providers often lack the technical capacity to participate in complex risk-sharing models, leaving a large portion of the Medicare population untouched by these innovations.
  • Policy Volatility: Frequent changes in CMMI leadership and strategic priorities can lead to a lack of continuity, making it difficult for providers to make long-term investments in model requirements.

The Role of Data and Predictive Modeling

To bridge the gap between projections and reality, there is a growing consensus that the CBO and CMS need more granular data. Avalere Health emphasizes that better predictive modeling—which incorporates provider-level behavior and regional market dynamics—is necessary to produce more accurate fiscal forecasts. Without a deeper understanding of how different types of health systems respond to financial incentives, policy projections will continue to miss the mark.

Policy Implications for Medicare Sustainability

The potential shortfall in CMMI-generated savings has significant implications for the long-term sustainability of the Medicare program. As the baby boomer generation continues to age, the pressure on the Medicare Trust Fund will only increase. If CMMI cannot serve as the fiscal stabilizer that lawmakers hoped for, Congress may be forced to consider more drastic measures, such as adjusting beneficiary premiums, altering eligibility requirements, or implementing more aggressive price controls.

The Need for a Refined Approach

Avalere suggests that instead of pursuing broad, one-size-fits-all models, CMMI should focus on more targeted interventions. By identifying specific populations or conditions where costs are highest and outcomes are poorest, CMS could achieve more meaningful savings. This approach would allow for more precise measurement of success and failures, leading to a more iterative and effective policy development process.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the primary role of CMMI in the Medicare program?

CMMI was established by the Affordable Care Act to test innovative payment and service delivery models that reduce spending while maintaining or improving the quality of care for Medicare, Medicaid, and CHIP beneficiaries.

Why does the CBO project savings for CMMI models?

The CBO projects savings based on the expectation that models will shift providers away from fee-for-service toward value-based care, which rewards providers for keeping patients healthy rather than for the volume of services performed.

Why does Avalere believe these savings are overestimated?

Avalere argues that the CBO’s models fail to fully account for the high administrative costs, provider attrition rates, and the significant difficulty of setting accurate financial benchmarks in diverse healthcare markets.

What happens if CMMI fails to meet its savings goals?

If CMMI does not generate the expected savings, policymakers may face increased pressure to find other ways to fund the Medicare program, which could include unpopular choices like increasing taxes or reducing benefits.

How can CMMI improve its future performance?

Experts suggest that CMMI could improve by focusing on more targeted, evidence-based models, reducing administrative burdens for participants, and utilizing more sophisticated data analytics to set realistic benchmarks for providers.

Conclusion

The Avalere analysis serves as a critical reality check for those who view CMMI as a panacea for Medicare’s fiscal challenges. While innovation is undoubtedly necessary, the road to sustainable savings is paved with complex operational hurdles that must be addressed. Policymakers must move beyond overly optimistic projections and embrace a more pragmatic approach to healthcare reform, one that prioritizes provider engagement, administrative simplicity, and data-driven policy design.
