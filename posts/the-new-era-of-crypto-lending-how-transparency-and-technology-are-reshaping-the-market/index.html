The New Era of Crypto Lending: How Transparency and Technology Are Reshaping the Market

2025-04-29

In the ever-evolving world of cryptocurrency, lending has emerged as one of the most powerful yet least understood sectors. Galaxy Research’s April 2025 report, The State of Crypto Lending: Bringing Transparency to an Opaque Market, provides a rare, in-depth view into how crypto lending has developed, the key players involved, and the pivotal changes reshaping the landscape after years of volatility, collapse, and rebirth.

Crypto lending, both onchain (DeFi) and offchain (CeFi), has become a major cornerstone in building liquidity and financial services atop digital assets. At its peak during the 2020–2021 bull run, the total crypto lending market ballooned to an astounding $64.4 billion. However, this size dramatically shrank following the catastrophic collapses of major centralized lenders like Genesis, Celsius, BlockFi, and Voyager, leading to an 82% contraction in CeFi lending alone. Today, while the total lending market remains 43% below its peak, it has shown signs of strong recovery, reaching $36.5 billion by the end of 2024, with DeFi leading the resurgence.

Centralized finance (CeFi) and decentralized finance (DeFi) represent two distinctly different approaches to lending. CeFi providers, such as Tether, Galaxy, and Ledn, operate like traditional financial institutions but in the digital asset space. Meanwhile, DeFi platforms like Aave and Compound have shown remarkable resilience and innovation, offering permissionless, transparent lending services powered entirely by smart contracts. The key distinction lies in accessibility, transparency, and operational risk: while CeFi requires traditional onboarding and can suffer from mismanagement or collapse, DeFi operates 24/7, with all terms coded into transparent protocols.

One of the critical findings of the report is how DeFi lending has overtaken CeFi by dominance. By late 2024, DeFi platforms accounted for 63% of the total crypto borrowings, nearly double their share from the 2020–2021 bull market. This shift highlights the growing confidence in decentralized infrastructure, especially after the failures that rocked centralized platforms. The explosion of DeFi lending has been attributed not only to transparency and programmability but also to the failure-resistant design of overcollateralization and automatic liquidation mechanisms that proved vital during market downturns.

The report also sheds light on the dramatic decline and slow rebound of CeFi. In contrast to DeFi’s 959% growth from the 2022 bear market bottom, CeFi remains heavily consolidated. As of the end of 2024, Tether, Galaxy, and Ledn alone controlled nearly 89% of the CeFi market, underscoring how the sector has narrowed to a few strong players following the crises of 2022–2023.

Risk mismanagement was at the heart of the CeFi collapses. Many lenders engaged in reckless practices, offering unsecured or poorly collateralized loans, using volatile assets like Grayscale Bitcoin Trust (GBTC) and stETH as collateral without proper liquidity strategies. Mining companies also suffered, as bitcoin mining equipment used for collateral lost as much as 90% of its value. These failures exposed the fragility of credit risk practices and poor internal controls that ultimately contributed to the sector’s near implosion.

Yet there is optimism on the horizon. Regulatory clarity, such as the SEC’s new policies, is encouraging traditional financial institutions like Cantor Fitzgerald and banks to cautiously enter the crypto lending space, bringing more credibility, liquidity, and risk management sophistication. Additionally, innovation in onchain private credit and tokenized debt markets is expanding the possibilities for future crypto lending models, blending the transparency of blockchain with the flexibility of traditional credit.

The report also outlines how interest rates differ significantly between onchain and offchain markets. Onchain stablecoin borrowing rates, while more volatile, have increasingly converged with offchain OTC rates. Similarly, DeFi’s ability to offer leverage against Ethereum liquid staking tokens (LSTs) has opened new strategies, like “looping,” where users borrow ETH against staked ETH to enhance staking returns. These financial innovations hint at a future where DeFi lending could dominate even more, not just by volume but by complexity and institutional adoption.

Finally, the role of crypto-backed stablecoins such as DAI, USDS, and GHO remains a crucial piece of the market. Although the share of CDP (collateralized debt position) stablecoins relative to total stablecoin supply has shrunk, they continue to represent an essential mechanism for obtaining dollar liquidity without selling digital assets.

In conclusion, Galaxy’s report paints a vivid picture of a crypto lending industry that has weathered extraordinary storms and is emerging stronger, smarter, and more decentralized. CeFi may still have a role, especially with institutional backers, but DeFi’s resilience and programmability are increasingly setting the standard for the future. As crypto markets mature, transparency, risk management, and innovation will be the pillars upon which sustainable lending ecosystems are built. Those who adapt to this new era—whether through DeFi apps or reformed CeFi practices—will be poised to thrive in the next wave of digital finance.
