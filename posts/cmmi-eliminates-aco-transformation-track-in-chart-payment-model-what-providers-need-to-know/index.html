CMMI Eliminates ACO Transformation Track in CHART Payment Model: What Providers Need to Know

2026-05-06

Understanding the Shift in the CHART Payment Model

In a significant update to federal healthcare policy, the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) Innovation Center (CMMI) has announced the elimination of the Accountable Care Organization (ACO) Transformation Track within the Community Health Access and Rural Transformation (CHART) model. This strategic pivot represents a major shift in how the federal government intends to support rural health systems in their transition to value-based care. For healthcare executives, policy analysts, and rural providers, understanding the reasoning behind this decision and its long-term implications is essential for navigating the evolving landscape of Medicare payment models.

The CHART model was originally designed to provide rural hospitals and health systems with the necessary capital and regulatory flexibility to improve quality of care and reduce costs. By offering two distinct tracks—the Community Transformation Track and the ACO Transformation Track—CMMI aimed to address the unique disparities faced by rural communities. However, as the healthcare market shifts and existing models like the Medicare Shared Savings Program (MSSP) continue to evolve, the necessity of a dedicated ACO transformation track has been re-evaluated.

The Role of the ACO Transformation Track

The ACO Transformation Track was intended to provide rural ACOs with the financial support and technical assistance required to succeed in two-sided risk arrangements. Rural providers have historically faced significant barriers in participating in ACOs, including low patient volume, limited infrastructure, and the high cost of data analytics. The track offered prospective payments and increased flexibility to help these organizations bridge the gap between fee-for-service and value-based care.

Key goals of the ACO Transformation Track included:

  • Increasing the number of rural providers participating in Medicare ACOs.
  • Providing upfront investment capital to build care coordination infrastructure.
  • Aligning financial incentives to prioritize patient health outcomes over volume.
  • Reducing the financial risk associated with transitioning to value-based payment models.

Despite these noble objectives, the complexity of implementing such specialized tracks within the broader federal payment framework proved challenging. CMMI’s decision to discontinue this specific track suggests a move toward simplifying the options available to rural providers rather than creating niche programs that may complicate participation.

Why CMMI Decided to Eliminate the Track

The decision to sunset the ACO Transformation Track was driven by several factors, including administrative alignment, participation trends, and the maturation of other CMS initiatives. CMMI has indicated that the resources previously allocated to this track will be redistributed to better support the Community Transformation Track, which focuses on broader health system reform at the community level.

Administrative Simplification and Alignment

One of the primary drivers is the need for administrative simplicity. CMS has been under pressure to streamline its various models to reduce the burden on providers. Managing a multitude of distinct, overlapping tracks can create confusion and administrative overhead for rural hospitals, which often have limited staff to manage compliance and reporting requirements. By focusing on the Community Transformation Track, CMMI aims to consolidate its efforts and provide a clearer path for rural health transformation.

The Evolution of the Medicare Shared Savings Program

The MSSP has undergone significant reforms that make participation more accessible for rural and smaller ACOs. The introduction of the ACO REACH (Realizing Equity, Access, and Community Health) model and various changes to the MSSP benchmark methodology have effectively addressed many of the challenges that the CHART ACO Transformation Track was originally intended to solve. Consequently, CMMI determined that the ACO Transformation Track was becoming redundant.

Implications for Rural Healthcare Providers

For rural healthcare organizations that were considering or currently participating in the CHART model, this change necessitates a strategic re-evaluation of their value-based care participation. The elimination of the track does not mean that rural health is no longer a priority for CMMI; rather, it signifies a change in the delivery mechanism for that support.

Impact on Financial Planning

Providers that were relying on the specific financial incentives of the ACO Transformation Track must now pivot their financial strategy. This may involve exploring the MSSP or other CMMI models that offer similar benefits, such as prospective payments or risk-sharing arrangements. Organizations should perform a thorough financial impact analysis to determine how this change affects their revenue projections and long-term sustainability.

Transitioning to Alternative Models

Rural health systems are encouraged to look toward the Community Transformation Track as a viable alternative for receiving support. This track focuses on multi-payer alignment and community-wide health improvement, which can offer a more holistic approach to value-based care. Additionally, the ACO REACH model remains a robust option for organizations looking to advance their care coordination and risk-sharing capabilities.

The Future of Rural Value-Based Care

The elimination of the CHART ACO Transformation Track is a microcosm of the broader shift in federal healthcare policy. As CMMI continues to iterate on its models, the focus is clearly moving toward models that are scalable, sustainable, and less burdensome. The future of rural value-based care will likely be defined by a more unified approach, where rural providers are treated as integral components of a larger healthcare ecosystem rather than isolated entities requiring separate, niche programs.

Strategies for success in the new environment include:

  • Investing in robust health information technology to track quality metrics.
  • Developing strong partnerships with community stakeholders to address social determinants of health.
  • Prioritizing care coordination and chronic disease management programs.
  • Staying informed about upcoming CMMI announcements and model updates.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What is the CHART model?

The CHART model (Community Health Access and Rural Transformation) is a CMMI initiative designed to help rural communities improve the quality of care and reduce costs by transforming how rural health systems are paid for their services.

Why was the ACO Transformation Track eliminated?

CMMI eliminated the track to reduce administrative complexity and because other programs, such as the Medicare Shared Savings Program (MSSP) and the ACO REACH model, have evolved to better support rural providers, rendering the specific track redundant.

Does this mean the end of support for rural ACOs?

No. CMMI remains committed to supporting rural health transformation. Providers are encouraged to participate in the remaining Community Transformation Track or explore other existing ACO initiatives that provide similar financial and technical support.

How should rural hospitals respond to this change?

Rural hospitals should evaluate their current participation in value-based models, review their financial strategies, and consider transitioning to more stable programs like the MSSP. Engaging with healthcare consultants or legal counsel to understand the nuances of these transitions is highly recommended.

Will there be future programs similar to the ACO Transformation Track?

While CMMI has not announced a direct replacement, it continues to develop new models for rural health. Providers should monitor the CMS Innovation Center website for updates on future opportunities and pilot programs that align with their organizational goals.

Conclusion

While the elimination of the ACO Transformation Track within the CHART model may cause temporary uncertainty for some rural providers, it is ultimately a step toward a more streamlined and efficient value-based care landscape. By consolidating resources and focusing on broader transformation, CMMI is aiming to create a more stable environment where rural health systems can thrive. For providers, the key to success lies in agility, proactive planning, and a continued commitment to improving patient outcomes through value-based care arrangements.
