Avalere Analysis: Why CMMI Savings May Fall Short of CBO Projections

2026-05-08

Understanding the Discrepancy Between CMMI and CBO Projections

In the complex landscape of American healthcare policy, the Center for Medicare and Medicaid Innovation (CMMI) occupies a critical role. Tasked with testing innovative payment and service delivery models to reduce expenditures while preserving or enhancing the quality of care, the agency is often viewed as a primary engine for fiscal sustainability within Medicare. However, a recent analysis by Avalere Health has cast a shadow of doubt on the long-term fiscal impact of these initiatives, suggesting that CMMI is unlikely to generate the level of savings previously estimated by the Congressional Budget Office (CBO).

The CBO, which serves as the nonpartisan scorekeeper for federal spending, has historically relied on optimistic assumptions regarding the scalability and efficacy of CMMI-led models. As policymakers look for ways to extend the solvency of the Medicare Trust Fund, understanding why these projections may be misaligned is essential for future legislative planning.

The Role of CMMI in Medicare Reform

Since its inception under the Affordable Care Act, CMMI has launched dozens of pilot programs, ranging from Accountable Care Organizations (ACOs) to bundled payment initiatives. The fundamental goal has been to move the healthcare system away from fee-for-service models toward value-based care. The theory is straightforward: by incentivizing providers to focus on patient outcomes rather than the volume of services rendered, Medicare can curtail unnecessary spending while improving health markers.

However, the Avalere report highlights a persistent challenge: the difference between a successful pilot and a system-wide transformation. While specific cohorts of patients may see improved outcomes, the administrative costs, the selection bias of participating providers, and the difficulty of scaling successful models across diverse geographic and demographic regions create significant friction.

Why the CBO Projections Often Miss the Mark

The CBO’s scoring process is inherently predictive, but it relies on historical data that may not capture the nuances of modern healthcare delivery. Several factors contribute to the divergence between CBO forecasts and the real-world performance of CMMI models:

  • Selection Bias: High-performing providers are more likely to join voluntary models, making it difficult to determine whether savings are due to the model itself or the inherent efficiency of the participants.
  • Administrative Complexity: The costs associated with implementing new reporting requirements and compliance frameworks often offset the gross savings generated by the programs.
  • Market Saturation: As more providers enter value-based arrangements, the “low-hanging fruit” of cost reduction is quickly harvested, leading to diminishing returns for later participants.
  • Spillover Effects: The CBO often assumes that savings will radiate outward to non-participating providers, but evidence suggests these spillover effects are inconsistent and difficult to quantify.

Key Findings from the Avalere Analysis

Avalere’s research indicates that the structural design of many CMMI models creates a ceiling for potential savings. By analyzing the historical performance of various models, the firm suggests that the legislative assumption that CMMI will reliably produce net savings for Medicare is overly optimistic. Instead, the analysis points toward a future where CMMI serves more as an incubator for quality improvement rather than a primary tool for deficit reduction.

This shift in perspective is vital for stakeholders. If CMMI is not the silver bullet for Medicare costs, then Congress must look toward broader structural reforms, such as adjustments to eligibility, provider reimbursement rates, or supplemental coverage regulations.

The Challenge of Voluntary Participation

One of the most significant hurdles identified by Avalere is the voluntary nature of most CMMI programs. When participation is optional, the agency faces the ‘adverse selection’ problem. Providers who are already delivering efficient care are the first to sign up, allowing them to capture bonuses for outcomes they were likely to achieve anyway. Conversely, providers who could benefit most from operational transformation are often the most hesitant to take on financial risk. This dynamic creates a scenario where Medicare pays out significant bonuses without achieving the broad-based efficiencies that the CBO models anticipated.

Implications for Medicare Sustainability

The realization that CMMI may not be the fiscal panacea that was once hoped for has profound implications for the future of the Medicare program. As the baby boomer generation continues to age into the system, the pressure on the Medicare Trust Fund will intensify. If CMMI initiatives fail to bridge the gap, legislators will be forced to confront difficult choices regarding benefit structures and tax funding.

Moving Toward Mandatory Models

Some policy experts argue that the solution is to move toward mandatory participation models. By requiring providers to operate under value-based payment structures, CMS could theoretically eliminate selection bias and ensure that efficiency gains are realized across the entire healthcare ecosystem. However, mandatory models face intense political opposition from provider groups, who argue that such requirements stifle innovation and place undue burdens on smaller, rural, and independent practices.

The Future of Value-Based Care

Despite the sobering findings of the Avalere report, it is important not to dismiss the value of CMMI entirely. While the fiscal savings may not meet the CBO’s high-water mark, the agency has been instrumental in shifting the conversation toward patient-centered care. The data gathered through these models provides invaluable insights into how to better manage chronic diseases, reduce hospital readmissions, and improve the patient experience.

For healthcare providers, the lesson is clear: the transition to value-based care is not merely about meeting Medicare metrics; it is about building sustainable operational models that can survive in an environment where payment is increasingly tied to performance. Whether or not the government achieves its specific savings targets, the industry trend toward value is irreversible.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the primary conclusion of the Avalere Health report?

The report concludes that CMMI is unlikely to generate the substantial net savings for Medicare that were originally projected by the Congressional Budget Office, largely due to issues like provider selection bias and the limitations of voluntary participation.

Why does the CBO project savings for CMMI models?

The CBO projects savings based on the assumption that shifting from fee-for-service to value-based payment models will naturally reduce unnecessary utilization and lower costs per patient. However, these models often struggle to account for the real-world administrative costs and the difficulty of scaling programs.

What is ‘adverse selection’ in the context of Medicare?

Adverse selection occurs when providers who are already efficient choose to participate in value-based programs to earn bonuses, while less efficient providers stay out. This means Medicare pays for improvements that were already happening, failing to produce the intended cost-saving behavior changes.

Could mandatory participation solve the savings problem?

Mandatory participation could theoretically reduce selection bias and increase savings, but it faces significant political and practical challenges, including provider backlash and the risk of destabilizing smaller healthcare practices that may lack the infrastructure to handle downside financial risk.

What is the future of Medicare cost control if CMMI isn’t the answer?

If CMMI falls short of savings goals, policymakers will likely need to consider a combination of structural Medicare reforms, such as adjusting eligibility criteria, changing provider reimbursement methodologies, or exploring new funding mechanisms to ensure the long-term solvency of the Medicare Trust Fund.

Conclusion

The Avalere report serves as a necessary reality check for policymakers and healthcare stakeholders alike. While the ambition of CMMI to modernize Medicare through value-based care remains a noble and necessary pursuit, the fiscal expectations surrounding these programs must be grounded in realistic assessments of provider behavior and market dynamics. Moving forward, the focus should be on refining existing models to be more inclusive and effective, rather than relying on overly optimistic projections that may ultimately fail to secure the financial health of the Medicare program.
