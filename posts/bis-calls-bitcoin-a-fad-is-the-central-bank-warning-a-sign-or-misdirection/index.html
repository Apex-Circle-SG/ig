BIS Calls Bitcoin a Fad: Is the Central Bank Warning a Sign or Misdirection?

2025-05-13

As Bitcoin continues to gain global acceptance—from retail investors to sovereign adoption—the Bank for International Settlements (BIS) has renewed its skepticism by calling Bitcoin a “fad”. This statement has sparked intense debate across the financial, crypto, and policy-making communities. Is this just more central bank resistance to decentralization, or is there a legitimate warning in the BIS’s stance?

This article breaks down the BIS’s criticism of Bitcoin, explores whether these concerns are grounded or outdated, and evaluates what this means for the future of decentralized finance (DeFi), institutional investment, and global monetary policy.

What Is the Bank for International Settlements (BIS)?

H2: Understanding the Global Central Bank of Central Banks

The Bank for International Settlements (BIS) is often referred to as the “central bank for central banks.” Established in 1930 and headquartered in Basel, Switzerland, BIS fosters global monetary and financial cooperation and acts as a bank for central banks.

Its influence spans:

  • Policy research
  • Regulatory frameworks
  • Financial stability monitoring

When BIS speaks, central banks around the world tend to listen—making its views on crypto assets particularly impactful.

The BIS Stance: Bitcoin as a “Fad”

H3: What the BIS Actually Said

In a recent publication and subsequent commentary by BIS General Manager Agustín Carstens, Bitcoin was described as a “speculative asset without intrinsic value,” emphasizing that its monetary system design is inherently flawed. Carstens further argued that cryptocurrencies are an “ephemeral fad” rather than a credible alternative to sovereign currencies.

H3: Key Criticisms from BIS

  • Lack of intrinsic value: Bitcoin, unlike fiat currency backed by state power or commodities like gold, is seen as purely speculative.
  • Scalability concerns: The BIS believes Bitcoin’s transaction speed and fees make it impractical for everyday use.
  • Environmental impact: The energy consumption of Bitcoin mining is cited as a systemic drawback.
  • Financial stability risk: BIS warns that mass adoption could weaken central banks’ ability to manage monetary policy.

Counterarguments: Why Bitcoin May Be More Than a Fad

H2: A Decade of Resilience

Bitcoin has outlived multiple obituaries, market crashes, and regulatory crackdowns. Despite volatility, it has consistently bounced back, and continues to hit new highs.

H3: Global Adoption Trends

  • Legal tender status in El Salvador and the Central African Republic
  • Major corporations such as Tesla, MicroStrategy, and Square holding Bitcoin in their treasuries
  • Spot Bitcoin ETFs gaining traction in the U.S., Canada, and parts of Europe

These signs point to growing institutional legitimacy, not decline.

H3: Digital Gold Narrative

Many investors now view Bitcoin as digital gold—a store of value during inflationary periods or geopolitical instability. Unlike gold, however, Bitcoin is divisible, portable, and resistant to physical confiscation.

H3: The DeFi Ecosystem

Bitcoin is only one piece of the larger decentralized finance puzzle. Innovations on Layer 2 networks (like the Lightning Network) are already addressing the scalability and speed concerns, while Wrapped Bitcoin (wBTC) plays a role in Ethereum-based DeFi.

Why Is BIS Opposed to Bitcoin?

H2: Protecting the Status Quo?

Critics argue that BIS, as a steward of the traditional monetary system, is naturally resistant to decentralization. Bitcoin challenges the very core of fiat monetary policy, offering a borderless, algorithmic, and finite alternative to government-issued money.

H3: Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs)

The BIS is a strong advocate for Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs)—state-backed digital tokens that offer some of the benefits of crypto but remain under centralized control.

Bitcoin, on the other hand:

  • Cannot be controlled or censored
  • Operates on a public, decentralized ledger
  • Limits supply to 21 million coins—making it deflationary by design

To BIS, Bitcoin is not just competition—it’s a revolution.

Public Reaction and Market Sentiment

H2: Investors Shrug Off the Fad Label

When the BIS statement circulated, Bitcoin’s price barely reacted. This is a stark contrast to earlier years when central bank criticism would lead to significant market dips.

In fact, some traders see such statements as bullish signals, interpreting institutional resistance as validation of Bitcoin’s disruptive power.

H3: Crypto Twitter Responds

Prominent crypto figures and influencers took to social media to mock the BIS remarks, posting memes like “Bitcoin is a fad—said the fax machine to the internet” or “Bitcoin is a fad… 15 years later.”

This reaction underscores the growing divide between legacy institutions and digital-native investors.

Is Bitcoin Really a Fad?

H2: A Fad Doesn’t Last 15+ Years

By definition, a “fad” is something that rises and falls quickly. Yet Bitcoin:

  • Has been around since 2009
  • Has weathered over a dozen major market crashes
  • Is held by millions globally
  • Is increasingly integrated into traditional finance infrastructure

Bitcoin has become an asset class in its own right. While its future utility as a daily currency may still be debated, its role in hedging, diversification, and digital sovereignty is becoming harder to ignore.

Final Thoughts

The BIS calling Bitcoin a fad is more a reflection of ideological tension than technical analysis. As the world experiments with new financial architectures—from DeFi to CBDCs—Bitcoin stands as a symbol of monetary freedom and technological evolution.

Whether you’re a believer or a skeptic, one thing is certain: Bitcoin is no longer just a speculative toy—it’s a global conversation.
