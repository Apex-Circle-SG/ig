GPU Not Detected or Showing in BIOS: Troubleshooting Guide for Windows Users

2026-05-02

Understanding the Issue: Why Your GPU Might Be Invisible

Experiencing a situation where your GPU is not detected or showing in BIOS can be incredibly frustrating. Whether you are a gamer trying to launch your favorite title, a video editor needing rendering power, or a professional relying on workstation performance, a missing graphics card renders your machine significantly less capable. When your computer fails to recognize the hardware, it is usually due to one of three categories: physical connectivity issues, BIOS configuration errors, or driver conflicts.

Initial Physical Inspection and Hardware Check

Before diving into complex software settings, you must ensure the hardware is physically sound. Start by powering down your PC completely and unplugging it from the wall.

Check the PCIe Slot Connection

The most common cause of a hidden GPU is a loose connection. Over time, the weight of modern graphics cards can cause them to sag, potentially pulling the contacts away from the PCIe slot. Carefully remove the card and reseat it. Listen for the audible click of the retention clip, which ensures the card is fully locked into place.

Verify Power Cables

Modern high-performance GPUs require dedicated power from your Power Supply Unit (PSU). Ensure that all 6-pin or 8-pin power connectors are pushed firmly into the card. If you are using modular cables, verify they are also securely connected to the PSU itself. A card that is not receiving sufficient power will often fail to initialize, resulting in it not appearing in the BIOS.

BIOS and UEFI Configuration

If the hardware is physically connected but still not showing up, the problem likely lies in your motherboard’s firmware. The BIOS is the first point of contact for your hardware, and specific settings can disable or hide expansion slots.

Accessing and Resetting BIOS

Restart your computer and repeatedly press the Delete, F2, or F12 key (depending on your motherboard manufacturer) to enter the BIOS menu. Once inside, look for an option to Load Optimized Defaults. Sometimes, an incorrect setting or a corrupted configuration can prevent the system from polling the PCIe lanes correctly.

PCIe Link Speed Settings

In some advanced BIOS menus, you can manually configure the PCIe link speed. If your motherboard is set to Gen 4 but your riser cable or card is struggling with the signal, it might fail to register. Try setting this to Gen 3 or Auto to see if the device becomes visible to the system.

Integrated Graphics Priority

Ensure that your BIOS is not forcing the system to prioritize Integrated Graphics. Look for settings labeled Primary Display or Graphics Adapter and ensure it is set to PEG (PCI Express Graphics) or Auto rather than IGD (Integrated Graphics Device).

Windows-Level Troubleshooting

If the GPU is visible in the BIOS but disappears once Windows loads, the issue is almost certainly related to your operating system or driver environment.

Using Device Manager

Open the Windows Device Manager by right-clicking the Start button. Navigate to Display Adapters. If you see an entry labeled Microsoft Basic Display Adapter or an Unknown Device with a yellow warning triangle, it indicates that Windows recognizes hardware is present but cannot communicate with it properly.

Performing a Clean Driver Install

Corrupted display drivers are a frequent culprit. Download the latest drivers from the manufacturer’s website (NVIDIA, AMD, or Intel). Use a tool like Display Driver Uninstaller (DDU) in Safe Mode to wipe all traces of the old driver, then perform a clean installation of the new software. This process removes registry entries that might be causing the conflict.

Advanced Troubleshooting: Testing the Hardware

If the previous steps have failed, you need to isolate the component. If possible, test your graphics card in another computer. If it fails to show up in a different system, the card itself may be physically damaged or dead. Conversely, if you have a spare GPU, plug it into your current motherboard. If the second card is detected, your primary GPU likely has a hardware fault.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why does my GPU show up in BIOS but not in Windows?

This is typically a driver-related issue. Windows is unable to initialize the device because of missing, corrupted, or incompatible drivers. Try performing a clean installation of your graphics drivers.

Can a faulty power supply cause a GPU to not be detected?

Yes. If your PSU does not provide enough wattage or if the specific rails powering the PCIe slots are failing, the GPU will not have enough power to perform its POST (Power-On Self-Test), making it appear as if it is not connected.

Should I update my motherboard BIOS?

If your hardware is relatively new, an outdated BIOS might not have the correct microcode to support your GPU. Check the motherboard manufacturer’s support page for BIOS updates that mention improved PCIe compatibility.

What is a PCIe riser cable and does it affect detection?

If you are using a vertical GPU mount, you are using a riser cable. These are common points of failure. Try connecting the GPU directly to the motherboard slot to rule out a faulty cable.

Final Thoughts

Troubleshooting a missing GPU requires a systematic approach, moving from the simplest physical checks to more complex BIOS and software configurations. By following these steps, you can identify whether you are dealing with a simple configuration error or a more serious hardware failure. Always remember to prioritize your safety when handling internal computer components and ensure your system is completely powered down before making any physical adjustments.
