Social Security Matters: Why Are My Spouse Benefits Less Than Half?

2026-05-17

Understanding the Basics of Spousal Benefits

Many retirees in the United States operate under the common assumption that Social Security spousal benefits always equal exactly 50 percent of their spouse’s primary insurance amount. While this is the maximum, it is rarely the reality for most applicants. If you find yourself in the position of asking why your payments are lower than expected, you are not alone. Understanding the nuances of Social Security rules is essential for effective retirement planning.

To qualify for spousal benefits, you must be at least 62 years old, or caring for a qualifying child under 16 or a disabled child. The social security benefit calculation for spouses is based on the primary insurance amount (PIA) of the higher-earning spouse. The PIA is the benefit your spouse would receive if they retired at their Full Retirement Age (FRA). If your own earned benefit is less than half of their PIA, you may be eligible to receive a top-up amount to match that 50 percent threshold. However, several factors often cause the actual payout to dip significantly below that target.

The Impact of Full Retirement Age

Why Timing Matters

Perhaps the most significant factor influencing your monthly benefit is your own Full Retirement Age. The Social Security Administration (SSA) determines your FRA based on your year of birth. For those born in 1960 or later, the FRA is 67. If you choose to claim spousal benefits before you reach your FRA, your benefit will be permanently reduced. The reduction is calculated on a prorated basis for every month you claim prior to your official retirement age.

For example, if you claim at age 62, the reduction can be substantial, sometimes lowering your monthly check by as much as 30 percent compared to what you would have received at age 67. It is important to note that unlike the workers benefit, spousal benefits do not increase if you wait until age 70 to claim them. Once you reach your FRA, your spousal benefit is effectively capped at that 50 percent mark. Waiting beyond your FRA offers no additional monetary advantage for spousal benefits, making timing a critical strategic decision.

The Role of Your Own Social Security Benefit

The Windfall Elimination Provision and Dual Entitlement

You cannot simply add your earned Social Security benefits to your spousal benefits to increase your total payout. Under the rule of dual entitlement, the Social Security Administration will always pay your own retirement benefit first. If your own benefit is lower than your potential spousal benefit, the SSA will then add a spousal supplement to bring your total payment up to that 50 percent mark of your partner’s PIA.

If your own Social Security record already provides you with a benefit that is more than half of your spouse’s PIA, you will not receive any additional money as a spouse. This often leads to confusion for couples who assume that they are entitled to both full checks. In reality, you essentially receive the higher of the two amounts, rather than a combination of both. This is designed to ensure a floor of financial stability rather than providing a windfall for high-income households.

Government Pension Offset (GPO)

If you worked for a local, state, or federal government in a position where you did not pay Social Security taxes, you might be subject to the Government Pension Offset. The GPO can significantly reduce, or even completely eliminate, your spousal benefits. The rule states that for every two dollars of your government pension, your spousal benefit will be reduced by one dollar.

This is a particularly frustrating rule for many public servants who find that their earned pensions interfere with their expected spousal benefits. It is vital to consult with a financial advisor or the local Social Security office to see how your specific pension plan will impact your potential future income.

Common Pitfalls and Misconceptions

  • Assuming the 50 percent is guaranteed: As discussed, this is a maximum, not a floor. Reduced claiming age and your own service record impact this figure heavily.
  • Forgetting about Medicare premiums: Your Social Security check is often reduced by your Part B Medicare premiums. If you feel your benefit is low, check if these insurance deductions are being taken out of your gross payment.
  • Miscalculating the spousal share: People often confuse their spouse’s actual check amount with their primary insurance amount. If your spouse delayed claiming their own benefits until age 70, their actual check is much higher, but your spousal benefit is still capped at 50 percent of their FRA amount, not their age 70 amount.
  • Filing at the wrong time: Filing before you are officially eligible or before reaching the FRA can lock in a lower benefit for the rest of your life.

Strategic Planning for Couples

Optimizing Social Security requires a coordinated approach. If one spouse has a significantly higher earnings history, the lower-earning spouse needs to consider their own work history and how long they intend to stay in the workforce. Sometimes, working just a few more years can bolster your own benefit enough that you no longer need to rely on the spousal supplement, or it may change the overall strategy for when the primary wage earner should file.

It is also helpful to create a my Social Security account on the official government website. This portal allows you to see your estimated benefits, review your earnings history for errors, and use calculators to model different scenarios. Having accurate data is the first step toward correcting any misconceptions regarding your monthly income.

Frequently Asked Questions

Question: Does my spousal benefit increase if my spouse delays their retirement?

Answer: No. While your spouse earns delayed retirement credits that increase their own benefit until age 70, the spousal benefit is calculated based only on their primary insurance amount—what they would get at their full retirement age. You get no extra benefit from their delay.

Question: Can I get spousal benefits if I am still working?

Answer: Yes, you can. However, if you are under your full retirement age, the Earnings Test may apply. This means that if you earn more than a certain amount annually, the SSA may withhold a portion of your benefits until you reach your FRA.

Question: What happens to my spousal benefit if my partner passes away?

Answer: If your spouse passes away, your benefit status transitions to survivor benefits. These are often higher than spousal benefits and can be worth up to 100 percent of what your spouse was receiving (or entitled to receive) at the time of their death. This is an important safety net for surviving spouses.

Question: If I divorce, am I still eligible for benefits on my ex-spouse’s record?

Answer: Yes, provided you were married for at least 10 years, are currently unmarried, and are at least 62 years old. The rules are very similar to ongoing marriages, but you do not need to wait for your ex-spouse to claim their benefits as long as you have been divorced for at least two years.

In conclusion, the complexity behind Social Security spousal benefits often leads to misconceptions. By understanding the influence of your own claiming age, the nature of dual entitlement, and specific offsets like the GPO, you can better navigate your retirement income. Always verify your numbers with the Social Security Administration directly and consider speaking with a financial planner to ensure your strategy aligns with your long-term goals.
