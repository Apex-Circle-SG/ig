Viridans Streptococcal Bacteremia in Choledocholithiasis: A Rare but Critical Association

2025-05-26

Viridans streptococci, a group of α-hemolytic, Gram-positive bacteria, are commonly found in the oral cavity and gastrointestinal tract. While they are typically commensals, they can become pathogenic under certain conditions, leading to infections such as endocarditis, liver abscesses, and, more rarely, bacteremia originating from the biliary tract. This article examines the rare but significant association between viridans streptococcal bacteremia and choledocholithiasis, a condition characterized by the presence of gallstones in the common bile duct.

Understanding Viridans Streptococci

The viridans group encompasses several species, including Streptococcus mitis, S. sanguinis, S. salivarius, S. anginosus, and S. gallolyticus. These organisms are part of the normal flora of the mouth and gastrointestinal tract. However, when the mucosal barriers are breached, such as during dental procedures or gastrointestinal tract perforations, these bacteria can enter the bloodstream, leading to bacteremia and subsequent infections.

Choledocholithiasis: A Potential Source of Infection

Choledocholithiasis occurs when gallstones obstruct the common bile duct, leading to biliary colic, cholangitis, or pancreatitis. The obstruction can create a stagnant environment conducive to bacterial growth. Infections in this setting are often polymicrobial, with enteric organisms such as Escherichia coli and Klebsiella pneumoniae being the most common culprits. However, cases involving viridans streptococci are exceedingly rare.

Case Reports and Clinical Evidence

A notable case report published in Cureus describes a patient with choledocholithiasis who developed bacteremia due to Streptococcus gallolyticus, a member of the viridans group. Magnetic resonance cholangiopancreatography (MRCP) revealed a 0.87-cm stone in the distal common bile duct, which was identified as the source of infection . This case underscores the importance of considering viridans streptococci as potential pathogens in biliary tract infections, even though they are not typically associated with such conditions.

Another study analyzed 51 cases of biliary tract infections caused by the Streptococcus bovis group, which includes S. gallolyticus. The study found that nearly half of the bacteremia cases had a biliary source, highlighting the potential for these organisms to cause significant infections in the biliary tract.

Diagnostic Challenges

Diagnosing viridans streptococcal bacteremia originating from the biliary tract can be challenging due to the rarity of such infections and the nonspecific nature of symptoms. Patients often present with fever, jaundice, and abdominal pain, which are common in various biliary tract disorders. Blood cultures are essential for identifying the causative organism, but the low virulence of viridans streptococci can lead to delays in diagnosis .

Imaging studies, such as MRCP or endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP), are crucial for visualizing the presence of gallstones and assessing the biliary tree’s condition. However, these imaging modalities may not always reveal the source of infection, necessitating a high index of suspicion and comprehensive diagnostic workup.

Treatment Strategies

The management of viridans streptococcal bacteremia associated with choledocholithiasis involves a combination of antimicrobial therapy and surgical intervention.

Antimicrobial Therapy

Viridans streptococci are generally susceptible to penicillin, making it the first-line treatment for infections caused by these organisms. In cases of penicillin resistance or beta-lactam allergy, alternative antibiotics such as ceftriaxone or vancomycin may be considered. Empiric therapy should cover common biliary pathogens until culture results are available.

Surgical Intervention

Addressing the underlying cause of choledocholithiasis is critical. Endoscopic or surgical removal of the obstructing stones is necessary to relieve the biliary obstruction and prevent recurrent infections. In some cases, percutaneous drainage may be required if endoscopic intervention is not feasible .

Prognosis and Outcomes

The prognosis for patients with viridans streptococcal bacteremia secondary to choledocholithiasis is generally favorable with prompt and appropriate treatment. Early identification and management of both the infection and the underlying biliary obstruction are crucial for preventing complications such as liver abscesses, septic shock, and multi-organ failure.

Conclusion

While viridans streptococci are not commonly associated with biliary tract infections, their potential to cause bacteremia in the setting of choledocholithiasis should not be overlooked. Clinicians should maintain a high index of suspicion for these organisms, especially in patients presenting with fever and signs of biliary obstruction. Timely diagnosis and a multidisciplinary approach involving antimicrobial therapy and surgical intervention are essential for optimal patient outcomes.
