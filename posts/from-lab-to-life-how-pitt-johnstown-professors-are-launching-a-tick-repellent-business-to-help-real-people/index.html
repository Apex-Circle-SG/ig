From Lab to Life: How Pitt-Johnstown Professors Are Launching a Tick Repellent Business to Help Real People

In the rolling hills of Western Pennsylvania, a quiet revolution is taking place within the laboratories of the University of Pittsburgh at Johnstown (Pitt-Johnstown). While universities are often known for theoretical breakthroughs published in obscure journals, a team of dedicated professors is breaking the mold. They are not just studying problems; they are commercializing solutions. Their latest venture? A groundbreaking tick repellent business born from a desire to answer a simple yet profound question: “How to help real people?”

As tick-borne diseases like Lyme disease reach record highs across the United States, the need for effective, accessible, and safe repellents has never been more critical. This article explores the journey of these academic entrepreneurs, the science behind their innovation, and the profound impact their business aims to have on public health.

The Growing Crisis of Tick-Borne Diseases

Before diving into the solution, it is essential to understand the magnitude of the problem. Tick-borne diseases have surged dramatically over the last two decades. According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), cases of Lyme disease have tripled in the United States since 1995. However, Lyme is just the tip of the iceberg. Ticks carry a myriad of pathogens responsible for illnesses such as Rocky Mountain spotted fever, babesiosis, and Powassan virus.

The traditional approach to prevention has relied heavily on chemical repellents containing high concentrations of DEET. While effective, these chemicals often come with downsides: skin irritation, toxicity concerns for pets and children, and damage to synthetic fabrics. Furthermore, many consumers are increasingly seeking natural or less harsh alternatives without compromising efficacy. This gap in the market—between high-toxicity chemicals and ineffective natural remedies—is exactly where the Pitt-Johnstown team saw an opportunity to intervene.

From Academic Research to Commercial Venture

The story begins in the biology and chemistry departments of Pitt-Johnstown. For years, faculty members have been studying local tick populations, analyzing their behavior, and testing various compound interactions. The pivot from pure research to business application was driven by a community-focused mission. The professors realized that keeping their findings locked in academic papers would not stop neighbors from getting sick.

The Catalyst: A Community Need

The driving force behind this enterprise was not profit, but protection. Rural communities in Pennsylvania are disproportionately affected by tick-borne illnesses due to the prevalence of wooded areas and wildlife. The professors witnessed firsthand the toll these diseases took on local families. This personal connection fueled the transition from “how does this work?” to “how do we get this to people who need it?”

By leveraging university resources, including the Innovation Institute, the team was able to navigate the complex landscape of intellectual property, safety testing, and initial production. This transition highlights a growing trend in higher education: the push for translational research that directly benefits the public sphere.

The Science Behind the Repellent

What makes this new tick repellent different from the dozens of sprays already on the shelf? The answer lies in a proprietary blend of active ingredients designed for maximum longevity and minimum toxicity.

  • Advanced Formulation: Unlike standard oil-based natural repellents that evaporate quickly, this formula utilizes micro-encapsulation technology. This allows the active ingredients to release slowly over time, providing hours of protection comparable to synthetic alternatives.
  • Safety Profile: Extensive testing has shown the formula to be safe for use on children as young as two years old and safe for use around dogs, a significant concern for many outdoor enthusiasts.
  • Eco-Friendly Ingredients: The sourcing of ingredients prioritizes sustainability, ensuring that the solution to one environmental problem (ticks) does not create another (chemical runoff).

The development process involved rigorous field testing in some of the most tick-dense areas of the Northeast. Students and faculty volunteers participated in blind studies, comparing the new formula against leading brand-name competitors. The results indicated comparable, and in some humidity conditions, superior protection times.

Challenges in Academic Entrepreneurship

Moving from the lecture hall to the boardroom is rarely easy. The Pitt-Johnstown team faced numerous hurdles that typically stall academic ventures.

Funding and Scaling

Securing initial capital for production runs is a classic catch-22 for startups. Unlike tech startups that can scale code infinitely, physical products require inventory, manufacturing, and logistics. The team utilized a combination of university grants, local business incubator support, and a successful community crowdfunding campaign to bridge the gap between prototype and product.

Regulatory Hurdles

Because the product is classified as a pesticide by the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), the regulatory barrier to entry is high. The team had to navigate a labyrinth of testing requirements and labeling regulations. This process, while tedious, ensured that the final product met the highest safety standards, reinforcing their commitment to helping real people safely.

The Impact on the Local Economy and Beyond

The launch of this business extends beyond public health; it is an economic engine for the Johnstown region. By manufacturing locally, the venture creates jobs in production, marketing, and distribution. It serves as a case study for other rural universities looking to revitalize their local economies through innovation.

Moreover, the project has invigorated the student experience. Students in business, biology, and marketing departments are now involved in real-world projects, gaining hands-on experience that textbooks cannot provide. They are learning about supply chain management, regulatory compliance, and brand storytelling.

Future Outlook: Expanding the Mission

With the initial product launch successful, the team is already looking ahead. Plans are in motion to expand the product line to include pet-specific formulations and treated clothing options. Additionally, a portion of the proceeds is being earmarked for a “Tick Education Fund,” which will support free community workshops on tick identification, removal, and disease awareness in underserved areas.

The vision is to create a national brand with a local heart, proving that effective public health solutions can come from anywhere, even a small campus in Western Pennsylvania.

Conclusion

The journey of the Pitt-Johnstown professors from researching tick biology to launching a full-fledged business exemplifies the best of what higher education can offer society. By asking “How to help real people,” they have moved beyond theoretical metrics to tangible life-saving results. Their tick repellent is more than just a product; it is a testament to the power of community-focused innovation. As they scale their operations, they carry with them the hope of reducing the burden of tick-borne diseases, one protected hiker, gardener, and child at a time.

For communities plagued by ticks, this local innovation offers a beacon of hope. It proves that with enough dedication and scientific rigor, we can turn the tide against nature’s smallest vectors of disease.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

1. Is the Pitt-Johnstown tick repellent safe for children?

Yes, the formula has been rigorously tested and is certified safe for use on children aged two and older. It avoids harsh synthetic chemicals often found in traditional repellents.

2. How does this repellent compare to DEET-based products?

In field tests, the repellent provided protection times comparable to DEET-based products but without the risk of damaging clothing or causing skin irritation associated with high-concentration chemicals.

3. Where can I purchase the new tick repellent?

The product is currently available through the university’s innovation store website and select local retailers in the Johnstown and greater Pittsburgh area. Distribution is expected to expand regionally in the coming months.

4. Are the ingredients environmentally friendly?

Absolutely. The team prioritized biodegradable and sustainably sourced ingredients to ensure that protecting humans does not harm the ecosystem.

5. How does this business support the local community?

Beyond creating local jobs, a percentage of profits goes toward a “Tick Education Fund” that provides free resources and workshops to schools and community centers in high-risk areas.
