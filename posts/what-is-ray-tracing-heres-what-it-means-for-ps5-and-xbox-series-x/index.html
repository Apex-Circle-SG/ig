What Is Ray Tracing? Here’s What It Means For PS5 And Xbox Series X

2026-05-01

Understanding Ray Tracing: The New Frontier of Gaming Graphics

In recent years, the term ray tracing has become a buzzword that defines the next generation of gaming. If you have been following the evolution of the PlayStation 5 and the Xbox Series X, you have undoubtedly heard how this technology is changing the way we experience virtual worlds. But what exactly is ray tracing, and why does it represent such a massive leap forward for console gaming?

At its core, ray tracing is a rendering technique that simulates the physical behavior of light. In traditional computer graphics, games have relied on rasterization, a process that approximates how light hits objects. While effective, rasterization often lacks the nuance of true-to-life lighting, reflections, and shadows. Ray tracing changes the game by calculating the path of millions of individual light rays as they bounce off surfaces, creating a level of realism that was previously reserved for high-budget Hollywood CGI.

How Ray Tracing Works

To understand the impact of ray tracing, it helps to visualize how light works in the real world. When you look at an object, your eyes perceive light that has bounced off that object after being emitted from a source, such as the sun or a lamp. Traditional game engines use shortcuts to simulate this, but ray tracing algorithms actually trace the path of light rays in reverse, from the camera lens back to the light source.

The Physics of Light

When these simulated rays hit an object, the software calculates how that light interacts with the material. Is the surface reflective like a mirror? Is it translucent like glass? Does it scatter light like matte plastic? By accounting for these variables, ray tracing produces:

  • Realistic Reflections: Surfaces like wet pavement, polished metal, and windows will accurately reflect the game environment in real-time.
  • Global Illumination: Light bounces off one object and illuminates another, creating soft, natural color bleeding that makes scenes look cohesive.
  • Accurate Shadows: Shadows become softer and more realistic as they move further away from an object, mimicking how light behaves in reality.
  • Ambient Occlusion: Crevices and corners receive accurate shading, adding depth and three-dimensionality to every scene.

Ray Tracing on PS5 and Xbox Series X

The introduction of hardware-accelerated ray tracing in the PlayStation 5 and Xbox Series X marked a turning point for consoles. For decades, console hardware lagged behind high-end PC gaming, but the custom RDNA 2 architecture found in these systems allows them to handle complex lighting calculations that were once impossible on a living room console.

Balancing Performance and Visuals

While ray tracing is a visual marvel, it is also computationally expensive. This is why many next-gen games offer players a choice between Fidelity Mode and Performance Mode. Fidelity mode usually keeps ray tracing enabled at a lower frame rate, while Performance mode might dial back the lighting effects to ensure a smooth 60 or 120 frames per second. This flexibility allows gamers to prioritize what matters most to them, whether it is the cinematic immersion of realistic light or the responsive feel of high-speed gameplay.

Impact on Game Design

Developers are no longer just using ray tracing for pretty visuals; they are using it to influence gameplay. For example, in horror games, dynamic lighting can create genuine tension as players navigate dark corridors where shadows shift realistically. In open-world titles, the way the sun reflects off water or skyscrapers adds a layer of scale and immersion that makes the world feel alive.

Why Ray Tracing Matters for the Future

As developers become more comfortable with the hardware, we will see ray tracing become the standard rather than the exception. We are moving toward a future where the line between pre-rendered cinematics and actual gameplay becomes nearly invisible. This technology is not just about making games look ‘prettier’; it is about creating a more believable and immersive experience for the player.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does ray tracing affect my game’s frame rate?

Yes, because ray tracing requires significant processing power, it can lead to a lower frame rate. However, modern consoles use sophisticated upscaling techniques like FSR (FidelityFX Super Resolution) to help maintain a smooth experience while keeping advanced lighting enabled.

Is ray tracing only for high-end monitors?

No, you can enjoy the benefits of ray tracing on any display, including standard 4K TVs. The technology is handled by the console’s GPU, so the visual improvements are baked into the game’s output regardless of the screen you are using.

Will every game feature ray tracing?

Not necessarily. While the consoles support it, it is up to the developers to implement it. Some games may skip ray tracing to focus on higher frame rates or more complex geometry, while others will use it heavily to define their visual style.

Can I turn ray tracing off?

In most games that support ray tracing, there is a settings menu that allows you to toggle it on or off. If you find that the game feels sluggish or you prefer a higher frame rate, you can usually disable ray tracing to prioritize performance.

Does ray tracing work in every lighting scenario?

Ray tracing is most noticeable in scenes with complex lighting, such as night-time city streets with neon signs, indoor environments with multiple light sources, or areas with water and reflective surfaces. It provides the most value in these specific environments.
