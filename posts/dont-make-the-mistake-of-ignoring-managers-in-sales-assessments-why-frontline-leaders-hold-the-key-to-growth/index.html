Don’t Make The Mistake Of Ignoring Managers In Sales Assessments: Why Frontline Leaders Hold the Key to Growth

When organizations look to improve revenue performance, their gaze almost always turns to the frontline sales team. We analyze their call scripts, evaluate their closing techniques, audit their CRM usage, and implement rigorous sales assessments to identify skill gaps. Yet, in this relentless pursuit of individual representative optimization, a critical blind spot often emerges: ignoring managers in sales assessments.

By focusing exclusively on the reps, companies inadvertently treat the symptoms of poor performance while ignoring the root cause of systemic issues. In the high-stakes world of modern B2B sales, the frontline manager is not just an administrator; they are the architect of your sales engine. If they aren’t assessed, coached, and aligned, your entire strategy is built on a shaky foundation.

The Multiplier Effect: Why Managers Matter More Than You Think

The role of a sales manager has shifted dramatically in the last decade. They have moved from being glorified top-performing reps to becoming strategic coaches, talent developers, and data interpreters. When you ignore managers in your evaluation processes, you fail to measure the very people who dictate the output of your entire team.

The Direct Correlation Between Leadership and Revenue

Research consistently shows that effective sales coaching is the single highest-impact activity a manager performs. A manager who can accurately identify a rep’s specific struggle—whether it’s discovery, objection handling, or negotiation—and provide targeted interventions will always produce a higher-performing team than a manager who simply focuses on “hustle.”

Identifying the ‘Managerial Gap’

Without including managers in your sales assessments, you miss critical data points, such as:

  • Coaching Proficiency: Can they actually teach, or do they just tell?
  • Pipeline Management Accuracy: Are they accurately forecasting, or are they allowing ‘happy ears’ to cloud their judgment?
  • Emotional Intelligence (EQ): Can they manage morale during slumps or maintain focus during spikes?
  • Strategic Thinking: Do they understand how their team aligns with broader company objectives?

The Perils of ‘Rep-Only’ Assessment Models

Many organizations rely on a “rep-centric” assessment model, which assumes that if you fix the bottom 20% of reps and push the middle 60%, the revenue will follow. This is a flawed assumption because it treats sales as a modular process rather than a dynamic, human-led environment.

When Reps Are Trained But Managers Are Not

Imagine you invest a six-figure sum in a state-of-the-art sales methodology training for your reps. They learn the new system, they are energized, and they return to the field. However, their managers never received the same training. The managers don’t understand the new terminology, they don’t know how to inspect the new pipeline stages, and they eventually discourage the reps from using the new system simply because it doesn’t align with their existing reporting metrics. You have wasted your training investment because you failed to assess and align the managerial layer.

What to Include in Your Sales Manager Assessments

To stop making the mistake of ignoring your leaders, you need a holistic assessment framework. Your manager assessment should evaluate both functional competency and leadership aptitude.

1. Coaching and Development Metrics

Don’t just look at their team’s quota attainment. Look at the variance in performance. Does the manager lift the low performers? Do they challenge the top performers to grow? A good assessment measures the manager’s ability to drive improvement across the entire spectrum of their direct reports.

2. Pipeline Inspection and Forecasting Rigor

Assess their ability to conduct a deal review. Can they strip away the ‘fluff’ and get to the reality of the deal’s health? A manager who cannot accurately assess their team’s pipeline is a liability to the CFO and the executive team.

3. Soft Skills and Culture

Frontline leaders set the tone. Use 360-degree feedback tools to assess their approachability, clarity in communication, and ability to advocate for their team when things get difficult.

How to Successfully Integrate Managers into Assessments

Moving from a rep-centric to a balanced assessment model requires intentional planning. Here is a strategy for integration:

  • Align KPIs: Ensure manager metrics are tied directly to the coaching activities that produce outcomes, not just the final revenue number.
  • Dual-Track Feedback: Include managers in the assessment process as both evaluators and the evaluated. When a manager assesses a rep, hold the manager accountable for the quality and accuracy of that assessment.
  • Baseline Competency Mapping: Just as you map a rep’s skills, map your manager’s leadership competencies. Do they have a gap in financial acumen? Do they struggle with difficult conversations?

Conclusion: Leadership is a Competitive Advantage

Ignoring managers in sales assessments isn’t just an oversight; it’s a strategic failure. Your sales representatives will only ever perform as well as the leadership they receive. By treating sales leadership as a measurable, improvable asset, you turn your management layer into a competitive advantage. The next time you sit down to assess your sales organization, start at the top. The return on investment for upgrading your management team is exponentially higher than focusing on the individual contributor alone.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Why do many companies ignore managers in sales assessments?

Often, it is a byproduct of prioritizing short-term revenue. Companies focus on the ‘doers’ (reps) to close immediate gaps, forgetting that the ‘directors’ (managers) determine the long-term sustainability of the revenue engine.

How often should managers be assessed?

At a minimum, biannually. However, in fast-paced environments, quarterly mini-assessments that focus on specific coaching outcomes are highly effective for driving performance alignment.

Can a sales manager be a good coach even if they weren’t a top-performing rep?

Absolutely. Coaching is a distinct skill set from closing. Some of the most successful managers were B-players who possessed high emotional intelligence and analytical rigor—traits that are often more valuable in a manager than raw closing ability.

What is the most important trait to assess in a sales manager?

The ability to diagnose. A great sales manager can identify exactly why a rep is failing (or succeeding) based on evidence, not intuition. Assessing their ability to ‘see’ the problem is the foundation of effective management.

Should I involve peers in my manager assessments?

Yes. 360-degree feedback is essential for managers. Because they bridge the gap between upper management and the frontline, understanding how they are perceived from both directions provides the most accurate view of their effectiveness.
