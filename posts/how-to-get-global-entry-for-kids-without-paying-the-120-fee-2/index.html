How to Get Global Entry for Kids—Without Paying the $120 Fee

2026-05-16

Why Families Need Global Entry More Than Ever

Traveling internationally with children is often described as one of the most rewarding yet challenging experiences a family can undertake. Between managing luggage, keeping little ones entertained, and navigating complex airport security lines, the stress levels can skyrocket before you even board your flight. For frequent flyers, Global Entry has long been the gold standard for bypassing long immigration queues upon returning to the United States. However, many parents hesitate to enroll their children due to the $120 application fee per child. The good news is that there is a proven strategy to waive this cost entirely, allowing your entire family to enjoy expedited processing without breaking the bank.

Global Entry is a U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP) program that allows expedited clearance for pre-approved, low-risk travelers upon arrival in the United States. While the program is famous for its TSAPreCheck benefits, which allow travelers to keep shoes and light jackets on and leave laptops in bags, the primary benefit for international families is the ability to skip the often hour-long passport control lines. Instead of waiting in the general population queue, Global Entry members use automated kiosks to scan their passports and fingerprints, printing a receipt that allows them to walk straight through to baggage claim. When you are traveling with toddlers or multiple children, saving thirty to sixty minutes at the border is not just a convenience; it is a necessity.

The Hidden Perk of Premium Travel Credit Cards

The secret to getting Global Entry for your children without paying the standard fee lies in the world of premium travel credit cards. Many high-end credit cards issued by major banks offer a statement credit of up to $100 or $120 every four to five years specifically for Global Entry or TSA PreCheck application fees. While most cardholders know to use this benefit for their own application, fewer realize that this credit often applies to any charge made to the card for these programs, including those for dependents.

To execute this strategy effectively, you must first identify if your current credit card offers this benefit. Cards such as the Chase Sapphire Reserve, The Platinum Card from American Express, Capital One Venture X, and the Citi Prestige Card are well-known for providing this perk. It is crucial to read the specific terms of your card, as some limit the credit to the primary cardholder only, while others allow the credit to trigger for any Global Entry fee charged to the account. If your current card does not offer this, or if you have already utilized your credit within the eligibility window, consider applying for a new card that does. The annual fee of these premium cards is often offset entirely by the travel credits, lounge access, and the value of expedited travel for the whole family.

Step-by-Step Guide to Waiving the Fee

Once you have identified a credit card with a Global Entry fee credit, the process of enrolling your children becomes a straightforward financial maneuver. The key is ensuring the payment method triggers the automatic reimbursement. Follow these steps to ensure you do not miss out on the credit:

  • Verify Your Eligibility: Log in to your credit card portal or review your benefits guide to confirm the exact amount of the credit and the frequency (usually once every 48 to 60 months). Ensure your account is in good standing.
  • Complete the Application: Go to the official Trusted Traveler Programs (TTP) website. You must create a separate account for each child, even if they are minors. Fill out all required biographical and travel history information accurately. Any discrepancies can lead to delays or denial.
  • Pay with the Right Card: When you reach the payment section of the application, use the premium credit card that offers the fee credit. Do not use a debit card, a different credit card, or a prepaid card, as this will not trigger the reimbursement.
  • Wait for the Statement Credit: The credit is rarely instant. It typically appears on your credit card statement within a few days to a few weeks after the charge posts. Monitor your account to ensure the credit is applied correctly.
  • Schedule the Interview: Payment is only the first step. Every applicant, regardless of age, must complete an in-person interview. Once your application conditionally approves, schedule the interview immediately, as slots can fill up months in advance.

Important Considerations for Minor Applicants

While the financial aspect of enrolling children is solved via credit card perks, the logistical requirements for minors are distinct from adult applicants. Parents must be fully prepared for the specific rules governing minor Global Entry applications. First and foremost, parental consent is mandatory. During the application process on the TTP website, you will need to provide information about the child’s parents or legal guardians. If the parents are divorced or separated, it is highly advisable to have documentation ready that proves you have the authority to apply for the child’s passport and travel documents, although CBP primarily looks for the presence of a parent or legal guardian during the interview.

Furthermore, every child, including infants and newborns, must appear in person for their interview. There are no exceptions to this rule. This means that if you are enrolling a toddler, you must bring them to the enrollment center. The interviewing officer will take the child’s photo and fingerprints. For children under 14, fingerprints may not be required if they are too small to capture clearly, but the photo and interview are non-negotiable. It is also vital to note that Global Entry membership for children expires on their 18th birthday, at which point they must reapply as adults, though the same fee-avoidance strategy can be used again if they have their own eligible credit card or if a parent pays on their behalf using a card with remaining credits.

Maximizing Your Enrollment Center Experience

Scheduling the interview can be the most frustrating part of the process, particularly for families trying to coordinate schedules. Enrollment centers are often located at major international airports, and their hours may not align with typical school or work schedules. However, there is a powerful tool available called Enrollment on Arrival. This option allows conditionally approved applicants to complete their interview upon landing in the United States from an international trip. This is often the most efficient method for families, as it turns a layover or arrival time into a completed requirement without needing a separate trip to an enrollment center.

If Enrollment on Arrival is not an option for your specific itinerary, you should look for standalone enrollment centers located in downtown areas or office parks, which often have more availability than airport locations. Additionally, keep an eye on the appointment calendar; cancellations happen frequently, and new slots open up regularly. Some families find success by checking the scheduling system early in the morning or late at night when fewer people are searching for appointments. Being flexible with your location can also yield faster results; driving an extra thirty minutes to a less busy enrollment center can save you months of waiting.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use my credit card benefit to pay for multiple children?

This depends entirely on the specific terms of your credit card issuer. Some issuers, like American Express, typically allow the credit to apply to any Global Entry fee charged to the card, meaning you could theoretically pay for your spouse and children in separate transactions and receive multiple credits, provided your specific card product allows it. However, many cards limit the credit to one application fee every four or five years per account. If you have multiple children, you may need to split the payments across different family members’ credit cards that carry the benefit, or utilize the benefit over different years as their memberships expire.

At what age should I apply for Global Entry for my child?

You can apply for Global Entry for a child of any age, including infants. Since the membership lasts for five years, applying as soon as your child has a passport is a strategic move. Even if your baby does not walk or talk, they still must go through immigration control, and having them in the Global Entry line can significantly reduce the time you spend standing in crowded terminals with a stroller and diaper bag.

What documents do I need to bring to the interview for my child?

You must bring the child’s valid passport and, if applicable, their permanent resident card. If the child is under 18, they must be accompanied by a parent or legal guardian who can provide proof of guardianship. It is also highly recommended to bring the child’s birth certificate to establish the relationship between the child and the accompanying adult. If only one parent is present, having a notarized letter of consent from the other parent can prevent potential complications, although it is not always requested.

Does Global Entry for kids include TSA PreCheck?

Yes, absolutely. When a child is approved for Global Entry, their Known Traveler Number (KTN) is linked to their passport. When you book flights, you must enter this KTN in the passenger information section for the child. This ensures that the TSA PreCheck indicator appears on their boarding pass, allowing them to go through the expedited security lane with you. Without the KTN entered correctly, they will be routed to the standard security line, even if you have PreCheck.

What happens if my child’s application is denied?

If a child’s application is denied, the $120 fee is non-refundable. This is why ensuring the accuracy of the application is critical. Denials usually occur due to incomplete information, criminal history, or customs violations. If you used a credit card with a fee credit, you will still be responsible for the charge if the application is denied, and the credit card issuer will not reverse the charge since the service was attempted. However, denial rates for children with no criminal history are extremely low, provided all information matches their passport exactly.

By leveraging the right financial tools and understanding the specific requirements for minors, families can secure Global Entry for their children at no out-of-pocket cost. The investment of time in the application process pays dividends in reduced stress and saved time for years to come, making your international adventures smoother from the moment you land.
