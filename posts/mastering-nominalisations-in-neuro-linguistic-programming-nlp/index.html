Mastering Nominalisations in Neuro-Linguistic Programming (NLP)

2025-05-12

In the world of Neuro-Linguistic Programming (NLP), language is not just a tool for communication—it is a powerful force that structures our experiences, influences our emotions, and directs our behavior. One of the most impactful, yet often overlooked, linguistic structures in NLP is the nominalisation.

Nominalisations are a specific type of language pattern where dynamic processes (typically verbs) are turned into static nouns. While they are common in everyday communication, they have a significant effect on how people conceptualize experiences, express emotions, and view problems or possibilities. This article explores nominalisations in detail—what they are, how they work, why they matter, and how to use them strategically for clarity, influence, and transformation.

What Are Nominalisations in NLP?

In NLP, a nominalisation is defined as a verb or adjective that has been turned into a noun, thereby transforming an active process into a seemingly fixed “thing.” This often makes the concept feel more permanent or unchangeable than it actually is.

Examples:

  • “Decide” becomes “decision”
  • “Communicate” becomes “communication”
  • “Improve” becomes “improvement”
  • “Angry” becomes “anger”

While grammatically correct, this transformation shifts perception. What was once a fluid action now appears static and objectified. In the context of personal development and communication, this can significantly influence behavior and mental framing.

Why Do Nominalisations Matter in NLP?

Nominalisations matter because language influences perception, and perception shapes reality. When people use nominalised language, they may unconsciously:

  • View situations as fixed or outside their control
  • Remove the subject (doer) of the action
  • Avoid responsibility or clarity
  • Generalize complex issues into abstract ideas

For example:

  • “There’s no communication in this team” is vague and implies a static issue.
  • Reframed: “We’re not communicating clearly with each other” puts the responsibility back into a process involving people.

In NLP, the goal is to re-activate nominalisations—to convert abstract concepts back into processes or actions that can be observed, improved, or changed.

How to Identify Nominalisations

A simple NLP test:

“Can you put it in a wheelbarrow?”

If not, it’s likely a nominalisation.

Try comparing:

  • “Agreement” (nominalisation) vs. “We agreed” (active process)
  • “Happiness” vs. “Feeling happy”

Nominalisations tend to obscure who is doing what, making them useful in some contexts (like diplomacy or theoretical discussions), but problematic when clarity or change is required.

Common Nominalisations in Everyday Language

  • Love
  • Trust
  • Freedom
  • Success
  • Motivation
  • Discipline
  • Education
  • Management
  • Conflict
  • Understanding

Each of these can be unpacked into actions or behaviors that clarify meaning and promote change.

The Impact of Nominalisations on Communication

✅ When Useful:

  • In abstract or philosophical discussions
  • When seeking to depersonalize conflict
  • For expressing complex concepts in summary

❌ When Problematic:

  • In personal development (e.g., “I lack confidence”)
  • In goal setting (e.g., “I want success” without defining steps)
  • In therapy or coaching when clarity is crucial

Nominalisations can mask problems, reduce accountability, or limit actionability.

De-Nominalising: Transforming Abstract Nouns into Actionable Processes

One of the most powerful NLP techniques is to de-nominalise vague language into clear, behavioral terms. This involves:

Step 1: Identify the nominalisation

“There’s no trust in the relationship.”

Step 2: Ask clarifying questions

  • “Who specifically isn’t trusting whom?”
  • “What does trusting look like to you?”
  • “What needs to happen for trust to be rebuilt?”

Step 3: Reframe into a process

“We haven’t been showing each other consistent, honest behavior lately.”

This transformation shifts the narrative from a fixed ‘thing’ to a dynamic ‘process’ that can be acted upon.

Nominalisations in Coaching, Therapy, and Business

🔹 In Coaching:

  • Helps clients articulate vague goals like “happiness” or “fulfillment” into measurable actions.
  • Encourages ownership of internal states and behaviors.

🔹 In Therapy:

  • Breaks down complex emotional issues (like “grief” or “depression”) into understandable and treatable experiences.

🔹 In Business:

  • Clarifies objectives and promotes accountability (e.g., “Let’s improve performance” becomes “Let’s meet these weekly targets”).

Practical Questions to Challenge Nominalisations

  • “What specifically do you mean by that?”
  • “Who is doing what to whom?”
  • “How do you know when [nominalised word] is happening?”
  • “What would [noun] look like as a verb or behavior?”

These questions help people move from abstract to concrete, from passive to active, from confusion to clarity.

Final Thoughts

Nominalisations are everywhere in human language—and they play a powerful role in how we structure our inner world. In NLP, recognizing and reframing these abstract constructions is essential to effective communication, personal growth, and cognitive flexibility.

By mastering nominalisations, you don’t just become a better communicator—you become a better thinker. You learn to see where language limits your perception and how to re-engage with reality as something dynamic, responsive, and full of possibility.
