What Distance Should Be Used to Pattern a Shotgun? The Ultimate Guide

2026-04-29

Understanding the Importance of Patterning Your Shotgun

For many shooters, the shotgun is often treated as a point-and-shoot tool that does not require the same level of precision as a rifle. However, this is a dangerous misconception. To become a proficient hunter or clay shooter, you must understand exactly how your shotgun delivers its payload. This is where shotgun patterning becomes essential. Patterning is the process of testing your shotgun at a specific distance to see how the pellets distribute themselves on a target. Without this data, you are essentially guessing at your effective range and the capabilities of your choke system.

The Standard Distance for Patterning

If you are wondering what distance should be used to pattern a shotgun, the industry standard for most upland hunting and clay target shooting is 40 yards. This distance is considered the benchmark because it represents the outer limit of effective range for most standard shotgun setups. By patterning at 40 yards, you gain a clear picture of how your choke tubes are performing when the shot string has had time to open up.

Why 40 Yards is the Benchmark

The 40-yard mark provides the most critical information regarding your shotgun’s density. At this distance, you can evaluate whether your pattern is too thin, creating gaps where a bird or clay might pass through unharmed, or if it is adequately dense to ensure a clean ethical kill. If your pattern is not holding together well at 40 yards, it suggests that you need to adjust your choke constriction or perhaps switch your ammunition brand.

How to Properly Pattern Your Shotgun

Patterning a shotgun is a straightforward process, but it requires patience and consistency. You should treat this exercise with the same focus you would give to sighting in a rifle.

Step-by-Step Instructions

  • Select a safe range: Always use a dedicated shooting range with a proper backstop.
  • Use large paper targets: You need a target large enough to capture the entire shot string, typically 36 by 36 inches or larger.
  • Choose your load: Use the exact ammunition you plan to use in the field. Changing shell brands or shot sizes will change your pattern significantly.
  • Establish a steady rest: Use a sandbag or a shooting rest to ensure the gun is held perfectly still, eliminating human error as much as possible.
  • Aim at a central point: Place a small dot or bullseye in the center of your paper. Aim directly at this point for every shot to see where your point of impact is in relation to your point of aim.

Analyzing Your Results

Once you have fired your shots, it is time to interpret the data. You are looking for a few specific things when evaluating your pattern.

Pattern Density and Distribution

Look for a uniform distribution of pellets. You want a pattern that is centered around your point of aim. If the pattern is shifted significantly left, right, high, or low, you may need to adjust your cheek weld or have your stock fitted. Furthermore, check for holes in the pattern. If you can see large gaps where a clay pigeon could pass through without being hit, your load and choke combination is not effective at that distance.

Calculating Percentage

Many serious shooters use a 30-inch circle drawn around the densest part of the pattern to calculate the percentage of pellets that landed within that circle compared to the total number of pellets in the shell. This helps categorize your choke as Full, Modified, or Improved Cylinder based on real-world performance rather than just the label on the tube.

Distance Variations for Different Disciplines

While 40 yards is the standard, it is not the only distance you should test. Depending on your specific shooting discipline, you might want to adjust your testing range.

Upland Hunting and Skeet

If you are shooting skeet or hunting fast-flushing birds in thick cover, you are likely taking shots within 15 to 25 yards. Patterning at 40 yards might be less relevant here. Instead, perform your pattern tests at 20 yards to see how quickly your pattern opens up. This will help you decide if you should be using a more open choke for close-range work.

Waterfowl and Turkey Hunting

For turkey hunters, the game changes entirely. Because turkeys are often shot at longer ranges and require a very dense pattern to the head and neck, many hunters test their setups at 30 to 50 yards. Tight chokes and specialized heavy-shot loads are common here, and patterning is non-negotiable to ensure you do not wound the bird.

FAQ: Frequently Asked Questions

Is it necessary to pattern every shotgun I own?

Yes. Every shotgun barrel, choke, and ammunition combination performs differently. Even two identical shotguns from the same manufacturer may show slight variations in point of impact. You should always pattern the specific gun you intend to use.

Does shot size affect the pattern?

Absolutely. Larger shot sizes, such as BB or 2, will result in fewer pellets in the air and a different pattern density compared to smaller shot like 7.5 or 8. Always re-pattern your shotgun if you decide to change your shot size.

How many shots should I fire at each target?

It is recommended to fire at least 3 to 5 shots at different targets to get a representative average. One shot can be an outlier, but a group of shots will show you the true consistency of your shotgun’s performance.

What should I do if my shotgun shoots low?

If your shotgun consistently shoots low, check your stock fit. A stock that is too low at the comb can cause you to look down the barrel rather than along the rib, resulting in a low point of impact. You may need to add a comb raiser or seek professional stock fitting.

Can I use a cardboard box for patterning?

While a cardboard box can work in a pinch, it is much harder to see the distribution of the pellets compared to using high-contrast white paper or specialized patterning targets. Using proper paper targets will provide much better feedback for your analysis.

Conclusion

Knowing what distance should be used to pattern a shotgun is the first step toward becoming a more effective shooter. By testing at 40 yards for general purposes and adjusting for specific hunting scenarios, you can take the guesswork out of your shooting. Remember, a shotgun is a precision tool, and the more you know about how yours performs, the more successful you will be in the field or at the range. Take the time to pattern your gun today—your future targets will thank you.
