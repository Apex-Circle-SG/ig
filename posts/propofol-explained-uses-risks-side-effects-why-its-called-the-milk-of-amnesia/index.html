Propofol Explained: Uses, Risks, Side Effects & Why It’s Called the ‘Milk of Amnesia

2025-06-14

Propofol is one of the most commonly used drugs in modern medicine, yet few people outside of healthcare fully understand what it is, how it works, and why it can be both a miracle and a potential danger. Widely used for sedation and general anesthesia, Propofol has gained infamy in the media, particularly after being linked to high-profile cases such as the death of pop icon Michael Jackson. But beyond the headlines lies a substance that has revolutionized how doctors manage pain, anxiety, and consciousness during procedures.

This article breaks down everything you need to know about Propofol—from its clinical uses and mechanisms of action to side effects, risks, and safety protocols.

What Is Propofol?

Propofol, also known by the brand name Diprivan, is a fast-acting intravenous anesthetic. It is primarily used to induce and maintain general anesthesia during surgeries, medical procedures, or in intensive care units for sedation. Because of its distinctive milky white appearance, Propofol is often colloquially called the “Milk of Amnesia.”

First introduced in the 1980s, Propofol quickly became the sedative of choice for anesthesiologists due to its rapid onset and short recovery time. Unlike older anesthetics, patients who receive Propofol often wake up with minimal grogginess and nausea.

How Does Propofol Work?

Propofol works by enhancing the activity of the neurotransmitter GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid) in the brain. GABA is a natural brain chemical that inhibits nerve activity. When Propofol increases GABA’s effect, it slows down brain function and leads to drowsiness, sedation, or unconsciousness.

  • Onset of Action: Within 30–60 seconds of injection.
  • Duration: Typically lasts 5 to 10 minutes, depending on the dose.
  • Elimination: Metabolized quickly by the liver, making it ideal for short-term sedation.

Medical Uses of Propofol

  1. General Anesthesia
    Propofol is frequently used to start and maintain anesthesia during surgeries. It can be combined with other medications like opioids and muscle relaxants for comprehensive anesthetic management.
  2. Sedation for Procedures
    It is used during non-invasive but uncomfortable procedures like colonoscopies, endoscopies, or dental surgeries.
  3. ICU Sedation
    In intensive care units, Propofol is used for continuous sedation in intubated patients who require mechanical ventilation.
  4. Status Epilepticus Management
    In rare, severe cases of seizures that don’t respond to other medications, Propofol may be administered.

Side Effects and Risks

While Propofol is generally considered safe when administered by trained professionals, it carries some serious risks.

Common Side Effects

  • Dizziness
  • Low blood pressure
  • Slowed breathing
  • Pain at the injection site
  • Nausea upon awakening

Serious Risks

  • Respiratory Depression: Can slow or stop breathing, requiring ventilation.
  • Propofol Infusion Syndrome (PRIS): A rare but life-threatening condition involving muscle breakdown, heart failure, and kidney failure.
  • Allergic Reactions: Though uncommon, hypersensitivity can occur, especially in people with egg or soy allergies (as Propofol formulations often include egg lecithin and soybean oil).

Misuse and Controversy

Propofol is not a controlled substance in many countries, including the U.S., but its abuse potential is real. Because it induces euphoria and a dream-like state, some individuals have misused it outside of medical settings.

Most notably, the drug was involved in the 2009 death of Michael Jackson, who used Propofol as a sleep aid—an application that is extremely unsafe and outside of all medical norms. The incident highlighted the importance of strict medical supervision and proper usage protocols.

Safety Measures and Administration

  • Only for Professional Use: Propofol must be administered by trained healthcare providers with access to emergency airway management tools.
  • Monitoring: Patients under Propofol sedation must be closely monitored for heart rate, blood pressure, and oxygen saturation.
  • Dosing: The dose varies significantly based on age, weight, and procedure type. It is titrated carefully to avoid overdose.
  • Storage & Handling: Once a vial is opened, it must be used quickly to prevent bacterial contamination due to its fat-based emulsion.

Who Should Not Receive Propofol?

Not everyone is a candidate for Propofol. Individuals with the following conditions should alert their physician before administration:

  • Known allergy to eggs, soy products, or Propofol itself
  • Heart failure or severe hypotension
  • Disorders of fat metabolism
  • History of Propofol Infusion Syndrome

Alternatives to Propofol

While Propofol is highly effective, alternatives may be preferred in some cases:

  • Midazolam: A benzodiazepine used for mild to moderate sedation.
  • Etomidate: Commonly used for patients with heart problems.
  • Ketamine: Offers sedation with pain relief and is popular in emergency settings.

Conclusion: A Powerful Tool When Used Responsibly

Propofol has revolutionized sedation and anesthesia with its fast onset, short duration, and relatively smooth recovery profile. But its misuse or inappropriate administration can lead to fatal consequences. For patients, understanding how Propofol works and why strict monitoring is essential helps build confidence in medical care.

Whether you’re undergoing a minor outpatient procedure or a major surgery, knowing what to expect with Propofol can demystify the process and alleviate anxiety.
