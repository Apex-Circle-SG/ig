Why Are Garden Scents So Popular?

The rise of the tomato candle trend is part of a larger shift in home fragrance. People are moving away from artificial-smelling vanilla or overpowering floral scents in favor of grounded, natural aromas. The scent of tomato vine is nostalgic—it evokes memories of summer days, vegetable gardening, and fresh produce. It is a scent that feels clean without being clinical, and sophisticated without being stuffy. This shift toward “biophilic” home fragrance—scents that connect us to nature—is likely to stay popular for years to come.

FAQ Section

Why is the original viral tomato candle always sold out?

The original candle went viral on TikTok, leading to a massive surge in demand that exceeded the small-batch production capacity of the brand. Because many of these candles are handmade or use specialized materials, they cannot be mass-produced quickly, resulting in frequent stock shortages.

What does a tomato candle actually smell like?

A tomato candle does not smell like cooked tomato sauce. Instead, it smells like the green, crisp foliage of a tomato plant. It features notes of crushed leaves, wet soil, vines, and sometimes a hint of citrus or basil. It is a very fresh, “green” scent.

Are these alternatives just as good as the original?

In many cases, the alternatives are even better. Many luxury and boutique candle brands use higher-quality waxes and more complex essential oil blends than mass-market viral products. You might find that these alternatives offer a better scent throw and a cleaner burn.

How do I make my candle last longer?

To extend the life of your new candle, always trim the wick to 1/4 inch before every burn. This prevents smoking and ensures an even melt pool. Additionally, avoid burning your candle for more than four hours at a time, and keep it away from drafts to ensure the wax burns consistently.

Can I reuse the vessels?

Absolutely! Once your candle has finished burning, you can clean out the remaining wax by placing the vessel in the freezer for an hour and then popping out the leftover wax. Use the empty vessel as a chic planter for small succulents, a pencil holder, or a decorative jar for small trinkets.
