CMMI Eliminates ACO Transformation Track: What CHART Model Changes Mean for Rural Providers

The landscape of healthcare payment reform is shifting once again, marking a pivotal moment for rural health systems across the United States. In a significant strategic pivot, the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) Innovation Center (CMMI) has announced the elimination of the Accountable Care Organization (ACO) Transformation Track within the Community Health Acceleration through Rural Transformation (CHART) payment model. This decision is not merely an administrative adjustment; it represents a fundamental recalibration of how federal agencies approach value-based care in underserved communities.

For rural hospital executives, policy analysts, and healthcare leaders, understanding the implications of removing the ACO Transformation Track is critical. This move signals a transition away from complex, population-based risk models that may have proven too burdensome for smaller entities, toward more direct and sustainable support mechanisms. In this deep dive, we explore the reasons behind this decision, the specific impacts on the CHART model, and what this means for the future of rural healthcare sustainability.

Understanding the Shift: Why CMMI Removed the ACO Transformation Track

To grasp the magnitude of this change, one must first understand the original intent of the CHART model. Launched to test whether targeted investments and payment reforms could improve health outcomes and reduce costs in rural communities, CHART initially offered multiple tracks, including the ACO Transformation Track. This track was designed to help rural providers transition into Advanced Alternative Payment Models (APMs) by forming or joining ACOs.

However, the reality of implementing ACOs in rural settings presented unique challenges that CMMI could no longer ignore. The elimination of this track stems from several key factors:

  • High Administrative Burden: Rural hospitals often operate with lean staffing and limited IT infrastructure. The data reporting and care coordination requirements inherent to ACOs created an unsustainable workload.
  • Population Density Issues: ACOs rely on large patient pools to spread risk effectively. Rural areas, by definition, lack the population density required to make statistical risk adjustment viable without exposing providers to catastrophic financial loss.
  • Provider Hesitancy: Many rural providers expressed that the leap to two-sided risk models was too steep, leading to low participation rates that threatened the statistical validity of the pilot program.

By eliminating the ACO Transformation Track, CMMI acknowledges that a “one-size-fits-all” approach to value-based care does not work for rural America. Instead, the focus is shifting toward models that address immediate financial stability and infrastructure gaps.

The New Focus of the CHART Payment Model

With the ACO Transformation Track removed, the CHART model is streamlining its efforts to maximize impact through its remaining components. The primary emphasis is now on the Community Transformation Track and the Rural Hospital Transformation Track. These tracks offer a more tailored approach to payment reform that aligns better with the operational realities of rural healthcare.

1. Enhanced Flexibility in Payment Structures

The revised model places a heavier emphasis on total cost of care (TCOC) arrangements that are calibrated specifically for low-volume providers. Unlike the rigid structures of traditional ACOs, the new framework allows for:

  • Customized benchmarking that accounts for regional socioeconomic factors.
  • Upfront infrastructure payments to support technology upgrades and care management tools.
  • Reduced downside risk exposure during the initial years of participation.

2. Direct Support for Infrastructure

Rural hospitals often struggle with the capital costs required to transition to value-based care. By removing the complexity of the ACO track, CMMI can redirect resources toward direct grants and technical assistance. This includes funding for broadband expansion, telehealth integration, and workforce development—areas that directly correlate to improved patient access and retention.

3. Strengthening Community Partnerships

The pivot away from ACOs encourages a broader definition of “accountability.” Instead of focusing solely on physician-hospital alignment typical of ACOs, the CHART model now incentivizes partnerships with community-based organizations, social service agencies, and public health departments. This holistic approach addresses social determinants of health (SDOH), which are often the primary drivers of poor health outcomes in rural regions.

Comparative Analysis: ACO Track vs. Current CHART Framework

Understanding the difference between the eliminated track and the current framework is essential for strategic planning. Below is a comparison of how the two approaches differ in practice:

Feature Eliminated ACO Transformation Track Current CHART Framework
Primary Goal Transition to Advanced APMs Financial stability and community health improvement
Risk Model Two-sided risk (Upside/Downside) One-sided risk or limited downside with caps
Data Requirements Extensive real-time reporting Streamlined, phased reporting requirements
Target Audience Integrated delivery networks Standalone rural hospitals and critical access hospitals
Support Mechanism Technical assistance for ACO formation Direct infrastructure funding and SDOH grants

This comparison highlights a clear trend: the federal government is moving away from forcing rural providers into complex corporate structures and toward supporting their unique operational environments.

Strategic Implications for Rural Healthcare Leaders

The elimination of the ACO Transformation Track in the CHART payment model offers a reprieve for many rural leaders who were hesitant to commit to high-risk contracts. However, it also demands a shift in strategy. Here are three actionable insights for healthcare executives:

Reevaluate Your Value-Based Care Strategy

If your organization was planning to enter an ACO solely to qualify for CHART, now is the time to reassess. The removal of this track suggests that independent success in value-based care may require different partnerships, such as regional collaboratives rather than formal ACOs.

Prioritize Infrastructure Investments

With the CHART model likely increasing focus on infrastructure grants, hospitals should prepare robust proposals highlighting needs in telehealth, data analytics, and care coordination platforms. Demonstrating a clear link between these investments and improved patient outcomes will be crucial for securing funding.

Engage with Community Stakeholders

The new direction of CHART emphasizes community-wide transformation. Hospitals should proactively engage with local public health officials, non-profits, and social service agencies to build coalitions that can collectively apply for CHART opportunities and address SDOH.

The Broader Context: Federal Trends in Rural Health

This decision by CMMI does not exist in a vacuum. It aligns with a broader federal trend recognizing the fragility of the rural healthcare safety net. Over the past few years, hundreds of rural hospitals have closed or are at risk of closure. The traditional Medicare payment models, which favor volume over value, have disproportionately penalized low-volume providers.

By eliminating the ACO Transformation Track, CMS is admitting that the path to value-based care for rural America cannot mirror the path taken by urban academic medical centers. It is an admission that flexibility, simplicity, and direct support are more valuable than theoretical efficiency gains derived from complex risk models. This pragmatic approach is likely to increase participation rates in the CHART model, ensuring that the pilot program can generate meaningful data on how to best support rural communities.

Conclusion: A Pragmatic Step Forward

The elimination of the ACO Transformation Track within the CHART payment model is a significant development that underscores the evolving nature of healthcare reform. While it removes one avenue for participation, it opens the door for a more realistic, supportive, and effective framework for rural providers. By focusing on infrastructure, community partnerships, and tailored payment structures, the revised CHART model has the potential to deliver the stability and innovation that rural healthcare desperately needs.

For rural hospital leaders, this is a call to action. The era of trying to fit square pegs into round holes is ending. The future lies in leveraging models designed specifically for the unique challenges and strengths of rural America. As CMMI continues to refine its strategies, staying informed and adaptable will be the key to thriving in this new environment.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What is the CHART payment model?

The Community Health Acceleration through Rural Transformation (CHART) model is a CMS Innovation Center initiative designed to improve health outcomes and reduce costs in rural communities through targeted payment reforms and investments.

Why did CMMI eliminate the ACO Transformation Track?

CMMI eliminated the track due to the high administrative burden, lack of population density for viable risk pooling, and low participation rates among rural providers who found the ACO structure too complex and risky for their operational scale.

Can rural hospitals still participate in CHART without the ACO track?

Yes. The CHART model continues to offer the Community Transformation Track and the Rural Hospital Transformation Track, which provide alternative pathways for payment reform and funding.

How does this change affect rural hospital funding?

This change likely shifts the focus of funding from risk-sharing incentives to direct infrastructure support and grants aimed at improving care coordination and addressing social determinants of health.

What should rural providers do next?

Providers should review the updated CHART guidelines, reassess their current value-based care strategies, and begin building coalitions with community partners to prepare for upcoming application cycles focused on the remaining tracks.
