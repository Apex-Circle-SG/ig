Strategy Taxonomy and Classification

The skill organizes options strategies into five main categories based on market outlook and trading objectives:

1. Directional Bullish Strategies

These strategies profit when the underlying asset price rises. Key strategies include:

  • Long Call: Buy 1 call option with unlimited profit potential and defined risk equal to the premium paid.
  • Bull Call Spread: Buy a lower strike call and sell a higher strike call, reducing cost while limiting both profit and loss.
  • Bull Put Spread: Sell a higher strike put and buy a lower strike put, generating credit income with defined risk.
  • Cash-Secured Put: Sell a put option while holding cash to cover potential assignment, ideal for those willing to own stock at a discount.
  • Synthetic Long Stock: Buy an ATM call and sell an ATM put to simulate stock ownership with less capital.

2. Directional Bearish Strategies

These strategies profit when the underlying asset price falls. Key strategies include:

  • Long Put: Buy 1 put option for bearish conviction with limited risk and substantial profit potential if the stock declines significantly.
  • Bear Put Spread: Buy a higher strike put and sell a lower strike put, reducing cost while limiting both profit and loss.
  • Bear Call Spread: Sell a lower strike call and buy a higher strike call, generating credit income with defined risk.

3. Neutral/Range-Bound Strategies

These strategies profit when the underlying asset price remains within a specific range. Key strategies include:

  • Iron Condor: Sell an OTM call spread and an OTM put spread simultaneously, profiting from low volatility and range-bound markets.
  • Iron Butterfly: Similar to an iron condor but with the body strikes at the same price, ideal for very tight range expectations.
  • Short Straddle: Sell an ATM call and put at the same strike, profiting from minimal price movement but carrying unlimited risk.
  • Short Strangle: Sell OTM call and put at different strikes, offering a wider profit zone than a straddle.
  • Covered Call: Own 100 shares and sell an OTM call, generating income while slightly limiting upside potential.

4. Volatility Strategies (Volatility Long)

These strategies profit from significant price movement regardless of direction:

  • Long Straddle: Buy an ATM call and put at the same strike, profiting from large moves in either direction.
  • Long Strangle: Buy OTM call and put at different strikes, cheaper than a straddle but requiring larger moves to profit.
  • Long Guts: Buy ITM call and put, similar to a straddle but with different risk characteristics.

5. Advanced/Multi-Leg Strategies

These sophisticated strategies combine multiple options for specific market conditions:

  • Calendar Spread: Sell a near-term option and buy a same-strike far-term option, profiting from time decay and volatility changes.
  • Diagonal Spread: Similar to a calendar spread but with different strike prices, adding directional bias.
  • Ratio Spread: Buy one option and sell two options at different strikes, creating asymmetric risk/reward profiles.
  • Butterfly Spread: Buy one low strike, sell two middle strikes, and buy one high strike, profiting from price settling at the middle strike.
  • Jade Lizard: Sell an OTM put and an OTM call spread, eliminating upside risk if credit exceeds call spread width.
  • Broken Wing Butterfly: A modified butterfly with unequal wing widths for directional bias.
  • PMCC (Poor Man’s Covered Call): Buy a deep ITM LEAPS call and sell a near-term OTM call, simulating a covered call with less capital.

Decision Framework: Which Strategy to Use?

The skill provides a decision tree to help traders select the appropriate strategy based on their market outlook:

  • Strongly Bullish: Long Call, Bull Call Spread, or Covered Call
  • Moderately Bullish: Bull Put Spread or Bull Call Spread
  • Neutral/Sideways: Iron Condor, Iron Butterfly, Short Straddle, or Covered Call
  • Moderately Bearish: Bear Call Spread
  • High Volatility Expected: Long Straddle or Long Strangle
