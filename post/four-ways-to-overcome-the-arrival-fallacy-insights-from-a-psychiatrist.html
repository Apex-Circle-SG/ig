Four Ways To Overcome The Arrival Fallacy: Insights From A Psychiatrist

You know the feeling. You’ve been working toward a major milestone—a promotion, buying a house, finishing a degree—for years. You imagine that once you cross that finish line, everything will finally click into place. You will be happy, settled, and content. But when you finally arrive, that profound sense of fulfillment is strangely missing, replaced instead by a hollow feeling of, “Is this it?”

This common phenomenon is known in psychology as the Arrival Fallacy. Coined by positive psychologist Dr. Tal Ben-Shahar, it refers to the erroneous belief that reaching a specific goal will provide lasting happiness. As a psychiatrist, I see the arrival fallacy in patients across every walk of life—from high-achieving corporate executives to people hitting significant personal life milestones. The goal is reached, the celebration is brief, and the treadmill of expectations restarts immediately. Here are four evidence-based ways to break free from this cycle and find satisfaction in the journey, rather than just the destination.

1. Cultivate Process-Oriented Thinking

The core of the arrival fallacy lies in outcome-dependency. When our self-worth and happiness are tethered strictly to results, we essentially sabotage our own well-being for the majority of our lives. If you spend 99% of your time working toward a goal and only 1% celebrating it, and your happiness is contingent on that 1%, you are setting yourself up for failure.

To overcome this, you must shift your focus toward process-oriented thinking. This doesn’t mean ignoring goals; it means re-evaluating why you are pursuing them. Ask yourself:

  • Do I enjoy the daily tasks associated with this goal?
  • Does this pursuit align with my core values, regardless of the outcome?
  • Can I find intrinsic motivation in the act of learning or improving?

When you value the process, the “arrival” becomes merely a waypoint, not the end-all, be-all. You become resilient because your satisfaction is derived from your actions—which you control—rather than external results, which you often don’t.

2. Practice Radical Gratitude for the ‘In-Between’

In our goal-obsessed culture, we are trained to overlook the present moment in favor of the “future self.” We believe our future self will be more capable, more deserving, and ultimately happier than our current self. This creates a chronic state of dissatisfaction with where we are right now.

Radical gratitude is the antidote. It is the practice of acknowledging the value of the present, the struggle, and the mundane parts of your life that you typically rush through. Psychiatrically speaking, this helps regulate the dopamine reward system. Instead of waiting for a massive dopamine hit upon achieving a goal, you learn to derive smaller, more sustainable reward signals from your daily environment.

Try this: Each evening, instead of just tracking what you accomplished (a to-do list), track what you were grateful for during the process. This primes your brain to scan for positives in the struggle, not just the success.

3. Detach Self-Worth from Performance

A significant driver of the arrival fallacy is the subconscious belief that we must be “productive” or “successful” to be worthy of happiness. This is a cognitive distortion—specifically, a form of conditional self-esteem. We behave as if our worth is a fluctuating stock market value based on our latest achievement.

To overcome this, you must perform the difficult work of separating your identity from your achievements. Your achievements are things you *do*; they are not who you *are*.

Exercises to Detach Worth:

  • Audit your narrative: If you lost all your achievements tomorrow, would you still believe you have intrinsic value?
  • Engage in non-performance-based hobbies: Do something just for fun where you have no expectation of being “good” or “productive.”
  • Reframe failure: View failures not as a deficit in your worth, but as data points in the process of learning.

4. Redefine ‘Success’ to Include Well-being

If your definition of success is strictly external (money, status, accolades), you will inevitably succumb to the arrival fallacy because those goals are essentially infinite—there is always more money to make, higher status to achieve. You are running on a hedonic treadmill.

To break this, you must expand your definition of success to include internal metrics. True success should be sustainable. If your pursuit of a goal compromises your mental health, relationships, or physical well-being, is it truly a success? As a psychiatrist, I often ask patients to draft a definition of success that includes:

  • Quality of relationships.
  • Mental and physical health markers.
  • Time autonomy.
  • Sense of purpose beyond self-gain.

When you align your goals with a broader, more balanced definition of success, the “arrival” feels satisfying because the process was nourishing, not depleting.

Conclusion: The Destination is Always the Present

The arrival fallacy is a natural byproduct of how our brains are wired to crave reward and progress. However, it is a psychological trap that keeps us from experiencing genuine contentment. By shifting from outcome-dependency to process-oriented thinking, practicing gratitude, decoupling worth from performance, and broadening your definition of success, you can stop living for the future and start living in the present. Remember, you never truly “arrive”—you are always becoming. Make the process of becoming something you enjoy.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What is the arrival fallacy example?

A common example is a professional who works 80-hour weeks for years to get a promotion, believing they will finally be happy. Once they receive the promotion, they feel a brief moment of relief, followed immediately by anxiety about the new job’s requirements, realizing their level of happiness has remained largely unchanged.

Is it wrong to have goals?

Absolutely not. Goals are essential for human growth and motivation. The arrival fallacy is not about having goals; it is about tying your entire sense of happiness and self-worth to the future completion of those goals.

How can I tell if I am experiencing the arrival fallacy?

Ask yourself: “Do I feel like I am just waiting for something to happen so I can finally relax?” If you feel that your happiness is ‘on pause’ until you reach a specific outcome, you are likely caught in the arrival fallacy.

Can the arrival fallacy lead to depression?

While not a clinical diagnosis in itself, the chronic dissatisfaction and the feeling of emptiness that follows the realization of the fallacy can certainly contribute to depressive symptoms, burnout, and a loss of life satisfaction.
